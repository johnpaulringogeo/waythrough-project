Wyoming Housing Resources & Tenant Rights

Waythrough Project · Updated May 2026

Wyoming’s landlord-tenant rules come from the Residential Rental Property Act (Wyoming Statutes 1-21-1201 and following). It is a landlord-friendly state — no rent control and no statewide source-of-income protection — but it does include an implied warranty of habitability, so your home must be safe and fit to live in. Eviction moves quickly on a 3-day notice. This page covers the statewide framework, your deposit rights, and how eviction works.

Quick numbers to write down:
  • 211 Wyoming — free, 24/7 — dial 2-1-1 for rental help, shelters, and utilities
  • Legal Aid of Wyoming: 1-877-432-9955 · lawyoming.org
  • Fair-housing complaints (no state agency — file with HUD): 1-800-669-9777
  • Wyoming Community Development Authority: wyomingcda.com
  • HUD regional office: hud.gov/states/wyoming

Public Housing & Vouchers in Wyoming

Section 8 Housing Choice Vouchers and public housing in Wyoming are run by local housing authorities (in cities like Cheyenne, Casper, and Laramie), and the Wyoming Community Development Authority (WCDA) supports affordable-housing development statewide. To find the authority that serves you, use HUD’s PHA directory or read how to find your PHA. Each authority keeps its own waitlist, so apply to several. For income-restricted apartments, search HUD’s LIHTC database or read how to find LIHTC housing.

Source of Income & Rent Control in Wyoming

Wyoming has no statewide source-of-income protection, which means that outside of a local ordinance a landlord may legally decline to accept a Housing Choice Voucher. Federal fair-housing law still bars discrimination based on race, color, national origin, religion, sex, disability, and familial status — and because Wyoming has no state fair-housing agency, those complaints go to HUD. See our source-of-income protections guide. Wyoming also has no rent control, so there is no legal cap on how much rent can rise. For a month-to-month tenancy, a landlord generally must give at least 30 days’ written notice before raising the rent or ending the tenancy.

The Implied Warranty of Habitability

Even though Wyoming is landlord-friendly, the Residential Rental Property Act requires landlords to provide a habitable home — one that is reasonably safe, sanitary, and fit to live in, with working electrical, heating, and plumbing systems and hot and cold running water (unless you’ve agreed otherwise in writing). If your unit isn’t habitable, give the landlord written notice and a reasonable time to fix it; keep records, and get legal advice from Legal Aid of Wyoming before withholding rent so you don’t risk an eviction.

Emergency Rental Assistance in Wyoming

  • Dial 211 for the current list of rental, deposit, and eviction-prevention funds near you
  • Community Action agencies across the state administer rental and utility assistance — 211 can connect you
  • LIHEAP helps with heating bills, which matter a lot in Wyoming — see utility assistance programs
  • Local nonprofits and tribal programs run emergency funds; 211 keeps the current list

Wyoming Tenant Law: Key Protections at a Glance

Quick Reference: Wyoming

  • Source-of-income protection: none statewide — a landlord may decline a voucher unless a local ordinance says otherwise
  • Rent control: none — no statewide cap on rent increases
  • Rent-increase notice (month-to-month): at least 30 days
  • Habitability: implied warranty — the home must be safe, sanitary, and fit, with working utilities
  • Nonpayment notice: a 3-day notice to quit before the landlord can file
  • Security deposit: no statutory cap; returned within 30 days of move-out (or 15 days after you give a forwarding address, whichever is later; up to 60 days if there’s damage)
  • Self-help eviction: illegal — only a court and a sheriff can remove a tenant

Security deposits

Wyoming does not cap the security deposit, and a landlord must tell you in writing at the start if any part of it is non-refundable. After you move out, the landlord must return the deposit within 30 days, or within 15 days after you provide a forwarding address (whichever is later) — and up to 60 days if there is damage to assess — with an itemized statement of any deductions. Document the unit’s condition at move-in and move-out, and read how to recover your security deposit.

Eviction process & how long it takes

Self-help eviction is illegal — a landlord cannot change the locks or shut off utilities and must go through the court. The sequence (forcible entry and detainer, or FED):

  • Notice: for nonpayment, a 3-day notice to quit once rent is at least three days overdue
  • FED complaint filed in circuit court; you are served and given a hearing date, often quickly
  • Hearing — appear and raise any defenses (the rent was paid, bad conditions/habitability, improper notice, retaliation); if you do not appear, the landlord usually wins by default
  • Judgment and writ of restitution: if the landlord wins, the court issues a writ and a sheriff carries out the removal

From notice to removal, a Wyoming eviction commonly takes about two to four weeks. Act quickly — get help from Legal Aid of Wyoming (1-877-432-9955), and read how to avoid eviction.

Where to Get Help in Wyoming

Discrimination complaints: Wyoming has no state fair-housing agency, so file with HUD at 1-800-669-9777.
