New Mexico Housing Resources & Tenant Rights

Waythrough Project · Updated May 2026

New Mexico’s rental rules come from the Uniform Owner-Resident Relations Act (NMSA 47-8). Protections are moderate: nonpayment gets a short 3-day notice, security deposits are capped for shorter leases, and tenants have habitability remedies including rent abatement. There is no rent control and no statewide source-of-income protection. The state housing agency is the New Mexico Mortgage Finance Authority (MFA, also called Housing New Mexico). This page covers the statewide rules, the eviction timeline, and links to every New Mexico city we cover.

Quick numbers to write down:
  • 211 New Mexico — free, 24/7 — for any housing emergency anywhere in New Mexico
  • New Mexico Legal Aid: 1-833-545-4357 · lawhelpnewmexico.org

Public Housing & Vouchers in New Mexico

Section 8 Housing Choice Vouchers and public housing are run by local authorities — the Albuquerque Housing Authority, the Bernalillo County and Santa Fe authorities, and others. The New Mexico Mortgage Finance Authority (MFA) finances affordable apartments and allocates Low-Income Housing Tax Credits — search HUD’s LIHTC database or read how to find LIHTC housing.

Apply to several authorities at once. Use HUD’s PHA directory or our how to find your PHA and how to apply for Section 8 guides. City waitlist status is on the city pages below.

Rent Control & Source of Income

There is no rent control in New Mexico — no statewide cap and no city has one, so a landlord can raise the rent at renewal with proper notice. New Mexico also has no statewide source-of-income protection, so a landlord may decline a Housing Choice Voucher. Check whether your city has any local rule, ask your PHA for its participating-landlord list, and read our source-of-income protections guide.

Emergency Rental Assistance in New Mexico

  • Dial 211 to reach local emergency rental and prevention funds and your regional Coordinated Entry for shelter and rapid re-housing
  • The Low Income Home Energy Assistance Program (LIHEAP) through the Human Services Department helps with utility bills — see utility assistance programs
  • MFA and Community Action Agencies run prevention and homebuyer programs; the pandemic-era statewide program has ended, so check what is currently open
  • The Salvation Army, Catholic Charities, and St. Vincent de Paul provide one-time help across the state

New Mexico Tenant Law: Key Protections at a Glance

Quick Reference: New Mexico (NM)

  • Source-of-income protection: none statewide
  • Rent control: none statewide and in no city
  • Security deposit limit: 1 month’s rent if the lease is under a year (NMSA 47-8-18); interest may be owed on larger deposits
  • Deposit return deadline: 30 days, itemized
  • Eviction notice (nonpayment): 3-day notice (NMSA 47-8-33)
  • Eviction notice (lease violation): 7-day notice to cure
  • Habitability: repair remedies including rent abatement (capped at one month’s rent)
  • Self-help eviction: illegal (NMSA 47-8-36)

Security deposits

Under NMSA 47-8-18, if your lease term is less than one year, the deposit cannot exceed one month’s rent. For longer terms a larger deposit is allowed, but the landlord must pay you interest on any amount over one month’s rent. The deposit must be returned with an itemized statement within 30 days of the tenancy ending. See how to recover your security deposit.

Eviction process & how long it takes

Self-help eviction — lockouts, removing belongings, shutting off utilities — is illegal under NMSA 47-8-36, and the landlord must go to court. The sequence:

  • 3-day notice to pay or quit for nonpayment, or a 7-day notice to cure for a lease violation
  • Petition for restitution filed in metropolitan or magistrate court; you are served and given a hearing date, usually within a week or two
  • Hearing — appear and raise defenses (payment, habitability, improper notice)
  • Writ of restitution: if the landlord wins, the court issues the writ and the sheriff carries out the eviction

An uncontested New Mexico eviction commonly runs about two to four weeks from the notice. Appear at your hearing with your lease, receipts, and photos. Get help from New Mexico Legal Aid and read how to avoid eviction.

Where to Get Help in New Mexico

Free legal aid: New Mexico Legal Aid (1-833-545-4357) handles eviction defense, habitability, and discrimination.

State housing agency: MFA / Housing New Mexico for LIHTC, rental, and homebuyer programs.

Find your local PHA: HUD’s PHA directory or our how to find your PHA guide.

211 helpline: dial 2-1-1 or visit 211.org for rental help, shelters, and utility assistance.

HUD fair housing: file at hud.gov/reporthousingdiscrimination or call 1-800-669-9777.
