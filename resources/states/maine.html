Maine Housing Resources & Tenant Rights

Waythrough Project · Updated May 2026

Maine gives renters relatively strong protections. Source of income — including Section 8 vouchers — is protected under the Maine Human Rights Act, so a landlord cannot refuse you or advertise “no Section 8.” Maine also requires long notice before a rent increase (45 days, or 75 days for a big one) and gives tenants real habitability remedies. There is no statewide rent control, though Portland has its own. This page covers the statewide framework, your deposit rights, and how eviction works.

Public Housing & Vouchers in Maine

Section 8 Housing Choice Vouchers and public housing in Maine are run by MaineHousing and local authorities (such as the Portland Housing Authority and the Bangor Housing Authority). To find the authority that serves you, use HUD’s PHA directory or read how to find your PHA. Each authority keeps its own waitlist, so apply to several. For income-restricted apartments, search HUD’s LIHTC database or read how to find LIHTC housing. Because Maine protects source of income (below), a landlord cannot refuse you for using a voucher.

Source of Income Protection Under the Maine Human Rights Act

The Maine Human Rights Act protects source of income as a housing right, so it is illegal for a landlord to refuse to rent to you because you would pay with a Section 8 Housing Choice Voucher or other assistance — and illegal to advertise “no Section 8” or “no vouchers.” Complaints go to the Maine Human Rights Commission at (207) 624-6290. See our source-of-income protections guide.

Rent Control & Rent-Increase Notice

Maine has no statewide rent control, but it requires a long notice before any increase: at least 45 days’ written notice, and at least 75 days if the increase is 10% or more. The city of Portland has its own voter-approved rent control that caps annual increases and adds just-cause eviction protections, so if you live there, check the city’s rent board. Even elsewhere, an increase made to retaliate against you for asserting your rights is illegal.

Emergency Rental Assistance in Maine

  • Dial 211 for the current list of rental, deposit, and eviction-prevention funds near you
  • General Assistance through your town or city office can help with rent in an emergency — every Maine municipality runs it
  • LIHEAP / HEAP helps with heating bills — see utility assistance programs
  • Community Action agencies administer rental and stabilization funds; 211 keeps the current list

Maine Tenant Law: Key Protections at a Glance

Quick Reference: Maine

  • Source-of-income protection: yes — Maine Human Rights Act; vouchers cannot be refused
  • Rent control: none statewide (Portland has its own); rent-increase notice is 45 days, or 75 days for an increase of 10% or more
  • Nonpayment notice: 7 days, and you can stop the eviction by paying what’s owed
  • No-cause termination (at-will): 30 days’ notice
  • Security deposit: capped at 2 months’ rent (1 month if you are 62 or older); returned within 30 days (21 days for a tenancy at will)
  • Habitability: implied warranty — repair-and-deduct and rent-escrow remedies are available after notice
  • Self-help eviction: illegal — only a court and sheriff can carry out an eviction

Security deposits

A Maine landlord may not collect more than two months’ rent as a security deposit, reduced to one month if you are 62 or older. After you move out, the landlord must return it within 30 days under a written lease (21 days for a tenancy at will), with an itemized statement of any deductions. If a landlord wrongfully keeps it, you may recover double the amount plus costs. Document the unit’s condition at move-in and move-out, and read how to recover your security deposit.

Eviction process & how long it takes

Self-help eviction is illegal — a landlord cannot change the locks or shut off utilities and must go through District Court. The sequence (forcible entry and detainer):

  • Notice: for nonpayment, a 7-day notice to quit — and you can stop the eviction by paying everything owed before the deadline; for a no-cause termination of an at-will tenancy, a 30-day notice
  • Complaint filed in District Court; you are served and given a court date
  • Hearing — appear and raise defenses (the rent was paid, bad conditions, improper notice, retaliation); Maine recognizes a warranty-of-habitability defense
  • Judgment and writ of possession: if the landlord wins, a writ issues after an appeal period and a sheriff carries out the removal

Because of the notice, the court process, and habitability defenses, a Maine eviction commonly takes about one to two months. Get help from Pine Tree Legal Assistance, and read how to avoid eviction.
