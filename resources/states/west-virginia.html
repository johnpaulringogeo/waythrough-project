West Virginia Housing Resources & Tenant Rights

Waythrough Project · Updated May 2026

West Virginia is a landlord-friendly state, and one feature makes acting fast especially important: a landlord can often file for eviction without giving any prior notice. There is no rent control and no statewide source-of-income protection, but the deposit-return law has real teeth. This page explains the statewide framework, the fast eviction timeline, and where to get help right away.

Quick numbers to write down:
  • 211 West Virginia — free, 24/7 — dial 2-1-1 for rental help, shelters, and utilities
  • Legal Aid of West Virginia: 1-866-255-4370 · lawv.net
  • WV Human Rights Commission (fair housing): 1-888-676-5546

Public Housing & Vouchers in West Virginia

Section 8 Housing Choice Vouchers and public housing in West Virginia are run by local housing authorities (in cities like Charleston, Huntington, and Morgantown), and the West Virginia Housing Development Fund (WVHDF) supports affordable-housing development statewide. To find the authority that serves you, use HUD’s PHA directory or read how to find your PHA. Each authority keeps its own waitlist, so apply to several. For income-restricted apartments, search HUD’s LIHTC database or read how to find LIHTC housing.

Source of Income & Rent Control in West Virginia

West Virginia has no statewide source-of-income protection, which means that outside of a local ordinance a landlord may legally decline to accept a Housing Choice Voucher. Federal fair-housing law still bars discrimination based on race, color, national origin, religion, sex, disability, and familial status, and the WV Human Rights Commission enforces those alongside HUD. See our source-of-income protections guide. West Virginia also has no rent control, so there is no legal cap on how much rent can rise. For a month-to-month tenancy, a landlord generally must give at least 30 days’ written notice before raising the rent or ending the tenancy.

West Virginia Tenant Law: Key Protections at a Glance

Quick Reference: West Virginia

  • Source-of-income protection: none statewide — a landlord may decline a voucher unless a local ordinance says otherwise
  • Rent control: none — no statewide cap on rent increases
  • Rent-increase notice (month-to-month): at least 30 days
  • Eviction notice: often none required — a landlord can file a wrongful-occupation petition without giving you advance notice (unless your lease requires it)
  • Security deposit: no statutory cap; returned within 60 days (or 45 days after a new tenant moves in, whichever is sooner) with an itemized statement
  • Wrongful withholding: a landlord who keeps the deposit improperly can owe you 1.5 times the amount
  • Self-help eviction: illegal — only a court and an officer can remove a tenant

Security deposits

West Virginia does not cap the security deposit, but the return rules favor tenants: the landlord must return it within 60 days after the tenancy ends (or within 45 days after a new tenant takes possession, whichever is sooner), with an itemized statement of any deductions. A landlord who wrongfully withholds it can owe you one and a half times the amount improperly kept. Document the unit’s condition at move-in and move-out, and read how to recover your security deposit.

Eviction process & how long it takes

This is the part to watch. Self-help eviction is illegal — only a court and an officer can remove you — but West Virginia is unusual in that a landlord generally does not have to give you advance notice before filing. The sequence:

  • Petition filed: the landlord files a petition for summary relief for wrongful occupation in magistrate or circuit court — often with no prior notice to you
  • Summons: you are served and the hearing can be scheduled within just a few days
  • Hearing — appear and raise any defenses (the rent was paid, bad conditions, improper service, retaliation); if you don’t appear, the landlord usually wins by default
  • Judgment and writ: if the landlord wins, the court issues an order of possession and an officer carries out the removal

Because no notice is required and hearings come fast, a West Virginia eviction can run in as little as two to four weeks. Don’t wait — if you’re behind on rent or get a summons, contact Legal Aid of West Virginia (1-866-255-4370) immediately, and read how to avoid eviction.

Where to Get Help in West Virginia

Tenant help & legal aid: Legal Aid of West Virginia (1-866-255-4370) handles eviction and housing cases statewide.

Discrimination complaints: the West Virginia Human Rights Commission (1-888-676-5546) enforces fair-housing law.

Find your local PHA: HUD’s PHA directory or our how to find your PHA guide.

211 helpline: dial 2-1-1 or visit wv211.org for rental help, shelters, and utility assistance.

HUD fair housing: file at hud.gov/reporthousingdiscrimination or call 1-800-669-9777.
