Alabama Housing Resources & Tenant Rights

Waythrough Project · Updated May 2026

Alabama’s landlord-tenant rules come from the Uniform Residential Landlord and Tenant Act (Ala. Code Title 35, Chapter 9A). There is no rent control and no statewide source-of-income protection, so knowing the notice periods and your deposit rights matters. Nonpayment starts with a 7-day notice, your security deposit is capped at one month’s rent, and the landlord has up to 60 days to return it. This page explains the statewide framework, the eviction timeline, and where to get help.

Quick numbers to write down:
  • 211 Alabama — free, 24/7 — dial 2-1-1 for rental help, shelters, and utilities
  • Legal Services Alabama: 1-866-456-4995 · legalservicesalabama.org
  • Alabama Housing Finance Authority: ahfa.com
  • Fair-housing complaints (no state agency — file with HUD): 1-800-669-9777
  • HUD regional office: hud.gov/states/alabama

Public Housing & Vouchers in Alabama

Section 8 Housing Choice Vouchers and public housing in Alabama are run by local housing authorities (in cities like Birmingham, Montgomery, Mobile, and Huntsville), and the Alabama Housing Finance Authority (AHFA) supports affordable-housing development statewide. To find the authority that serves you, use HUD’s PHA directory or read how to find your PHA. Each authority keeps its own waitlist, so apply to several. For income-restricted apartments, search HUD’s LIHTC database or read how to find LIHTC housing.

Source of Income & Rent Control in Alabama

Alabama has no statewide source-of-income protection, which means that outside of a local ordinance a landlord may legally decline to accept a Housing Choice Voucher. Federal fair-housing law still bars discrimination based on race, color, national origin, religion, sex, disability, and familial status — and because Alabama has no state fair-housing agency, those complaints go to HUD. See our source-of-income protections guide. Alabama also has no rent control, so there is no legal cap on how much rent can rise. For a month-to-month tenancy, a landlord generally must give at least 30 days’ written notice before raising the rent or ending the tenancy.

Emergency Rental Assistance in Alabama

  • Dial 211 for the current list of rental, deposit, and eviction-prevention funds near you
  • Community Action agencies across the state administer rental and utility assistance — 211 can connect you
  • LIHEAP helps with heating and cooling bills — see utility assistance programs
  • Local nonprofits, churches, and the Salvation Army often run emergency funds; 211 keeps the current list

Alabama Tenant Law: Key Protections at a Glance

Quick Reference: Alabama

  • Source-of-income protection: none statewide — a landlord may decline a voucher unless a local ordinance says otherwise
  • Rent control: none — no statewide cap on rent increases
  • Rent-increase notice (month-to-month): at least 30 days
  • Nonpayment notice: 7 days to pay or quit (Ala. Code § 35-9A-421)
  • Lease-violation notice: 14 days to cure (7 days for a health/safety hazard)
  • Security deposit: capped at one month’s rent (pet, risk, and change deposits are separate); returned within 60 days with an itemized statement
  • Self-help eviction: illegal — the landlord must use the courts to remove a tenant

Security deposits

An Alabama landlord may not collect more than one month’s rent as a security deposit, though separate amounts are allowed for pets, for changes the tenant wants, and to cover an undue risk to the property. After you move out and provide a forwarding address, the landlord has 60 days to return the deposit with an itemized statement of any deductions for unpaid rent or damage beyond normal wear. Document the unit’s condition at move-in and move-out, and read how to recover your security deposit.

Eviction process & how long it takes

Self-help eviction is illegal — a landlord cannot change the locks or shut off utilities and must go through the court. The sequence (unlawful detainer):

  • Notice: for nonpayment, a 7-day notice to pay or quit; for a lease violation, a 14-day notice to cure (7 days for a health or safety hazard)
  • Unlawful detainer complaint filed in district court; you are served and generally have 7 days to file a written answer
  • Hearing — appear and raise any defenses (the rent was paid, improper notice, conditions, retaliation); if you don’t answer, the landlord usually wins by default
  • Writ of possession: if the landlord wins, the court issues a writ and a sheriff carries out the removal (often after a short period such as 7 days)

From notice to removal, an Alabama eviction commonly takes about one to two months. File your answer on time and get help from Legal Services Alabama (1-866-456-4995), and read how to avoid eviction.

Where to Get Help in Alabama

Tenant help & legal aid: Legal Services Alabama (1-866-456-4995) handles eviction and housing cases for income-eligible tenants.

Discrimination complaints: Alabama has no state fair-housing agency, so file with HUD at 1-800-669-9777.

Find your local PHA: HUD’s PHA directory or our how to find your PHA guide.

211 helpline: dial 2-1-1 or visit 211.org for rental help, shelters, and utility assistance.

HUD fair housing: file at hud.gov/reporthousingdiscrimination or call 1-800-669-9777.
