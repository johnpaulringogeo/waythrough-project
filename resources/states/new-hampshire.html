New Hampshire Housing Resources & Tenant Rights

Waythrough Project · Updated May 2026

New Hampshire’s landlord-tenant rules come from RSA 540 (and RSA 540-A). A useful protection here: for most rental housing (“restricted property”), a landlord can only evict for good cause — not simply because a lease ended. There is no rent control and no statewide source-of-income protection, but deposits are capped and notice rules are clear. This page covers the statewide framework, your deposit rights, and how eviction works.

Public Housing & Vouchers in New Hampshire

Section 8 Housing Choice Vouchers and public housing in New Hampshire are run by New Hampshire Housing and local authorities (such as the Manchester, Nashua, and Portsmouth housing authorities). To find the authority that serves you, use HUD’s PHA directory or read how to find your PHA. Each authority keeps its own waitlist, so apply to several. For income-restricted apartments, search HUD’s LIHTC database or read how to find LIHTC housing.

Source of Income & Rent Control in New Hampshire

New Hampshire has no statewide source-of-income protection, which means that outside of a local ordinance a landlord may legally decline to accept a Housing Choice Voucher. Federal fair-housing law still bars discrimination based on race, color, national origin, religion, sex, disability, and familial status, and the NH Commission for Human Rights enforces those alongside HUD. See our source-of-income protections guide. New Hampshire also has no rent control, so there is no legal cap on rent increases. For a month-to-month tenancy, a landlord generally must give at least 30 days’ written notice before raising the rent.

Good-Cause Eviction for Restricted Property

This is New Hampshire’s standout protection. For most rental housing — what the law calls “restricted property” (it excludes things like a unit in an owner-occupied building of a few units) — a landlord may only evict for good cause under RSA 540. Good cause includes nonpayment, a substantial lease violation, damage, or behavior affecting others, and also “other good cause,” which can include a legitimate business or economic reason. The key point: a landlord cannot evict simply because your lease term ran out, the way they can in many states.

New Hampshire Tenant Law: Key Protections at a Glance

Quick Reference: New Hampshire

  • Source-of-income protection: none statewide — a landlord may decline a voucher unless a local ordinance says otherwise
  • Good cause to evict: required for restricted property (RSA 540) — a landlord can’t evict just because a lease ended
  • Rent control: none — 30 days’ notice of an increase for month-to-month tenants
  • Nonpayment notice: 7 days — and you can usually stop the eviction by paying the rent owed plus $15 (once per year)
  • Security deposit: capped at one month’s rent or $100, whichever is greater; returned within 30 days with an itemized statement
  • Self-help eviction: illegal — RSA 540-A bars lockouts and utility shutoffs, with damages for violations

Security deposits

A New Hampshire landlord may not collect more than one month’s rent or $100, whichever is greater, as a security deposit. After you move out, the landlord must return it within 30 days with an itemized statement of any deductions for unpaid rent or damage beyond normal wear. RSA 540-A also protects you from lockouts and utility shutoffs — a landlord who does that can owe you damages. Document the unit’s condition at move-in and move-out, and read how to recover your security deposit.

Eviction process & how long it takes

Self-help eviction is illegal — a landlord cannot change the locks or shut off utilities and must go through the Circuit Court. The sequence:

  • Notice: for nonpayment, a 7-day eviction notice (Demand for Rent) — and you can usually stop the eviction by paying the rent owed plus $15, once in a 12-month period; for other good cause, often a 30-day notice
  • Landlord & Tenant Writ filed in Circuit Court; you are served and must file an Appearance to get a hearing
  • Hearing — appear and raise defenses (the rent was paid, no good cause, bad conditions, retaliation, improper notice)
  • Judgment and writ of possession: if the landlord wins, a writ issues after an appeal period and a sheriff carries out the removal

From notice to removal, a New Hampshire eviction commonly takes about one to two months. File your Appearance on time and get help from New Hampshire Legal Assistance (1-800-639-5290), and read how to avoid eviction.

Where to Get Help in New Hampshire

Tenant help & legal aid: New Hampshire Legal Assistance and 603 Legal Aid (1-800-639-5290) handle eviction and housing cases.

Discrimination complaints: the New Hampshire Commission for Human Rights ((603) 271-2767) enforces fair-housing law.

Find your local PHA: HUD’s PHA directory or our how to find your PHA guide.

211 helpline: dial 2-1-1 or visit 211nh.org for rental help, shelters, and utility assistance.

HUD fair housing: file at hud.gov/reporthousingdiscrimination or call 1-800-669-9777.
