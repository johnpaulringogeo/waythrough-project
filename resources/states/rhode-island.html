Rhode Island Housing Resources & Tenant Rights

Waythrough Project · Updated May 2026

Rhode Island’s landlord-tenant rules come from the Residential Landlord and Tenant Act (R.I. Gen. Laws Title 34, Chapter 18). Rhode Island now protects renters from source-of-income discrimination — a landlord can’t refuse you just for using a Section 8 voucher. There is no statewide rent control, but the law gives a 15-day grace period on rent and caps deposits at one month. This page covers the statewide framework, your deposit rights, and how eviction works.

Public Housing & Vouchers in Rhode Island

Section 8 Housing Choice Vouchers and public housing in Rhode Island are run by RIHousing and local authorities (such as the Providence, Pawtucket, and Woonsocket housing authorities). To find the authority that serves you, use HUD’s PHA directory or read how to find your PHA. Each authority keeps its own waitlist, so apply to several. For income-restricted apartments, search HUD’s LIHTC database or read how to find LIHTC housing. Because Rhode Island protects source of income (below), a landlord cannot refuse you for using a voucher.

Source of Income Protection

Rhode Island prohibits housing discrimination based on source of income under the state’s Fair Housing Practices Act, so it is illegal for a landlord to refuse to rent to you because you would pay with a Section 8 Housing Choice Voucher or other lawful assistance. Complaints go to the Rhode Island Commission for Human Rights at (401) 222-2661. See our source-of-income protections guide.

Rent Control & the 15-Day Grace Period

Rhode Island has no statewide rent control, so there is no legal cap on how much rent can rise; a month-to-month landlord generally must give 30 days’ notice of an increase. Rhode Island law does give tenants a 15-day grace period on rent — the landlord can only start the nonpayment process after rent is 15 days late, and then must serve a 5-day demand. Providence offers extra help, including mediation for some increases, a stabilization program for older buildings, and an eviction-defense program.

Rhode Island Tenant Law: Key Protections at a Glance

Quick Reference: Rhode Island

  • Source-of-income protection: yes — vouchers and assistance cannot be refused
  • Rent control: none statewide; 30 days’ notice of a rent increase for month-to-month tenants
  • Grace period: 15 days on rent before the landlord can act on nonpayment
  • Nonpayment notice: a 5-day demand after the grace period
  • Security deposit: capped at one month’s rent; returned within 20 days with an itemized statement
  • Self-help eviction: illegal — only the District Court and a constable can carry out an eviction

Security deposits

A Rhode Island landlord may not collect more than one month’s rent as a security deposit. After you move out and give a forwarding address, the landlord must return it within 20 days, along with an itemized statement of any deductions for unpaid rent or damage beyond normal wear. Document the unit’s condition at move-in and move-out, and read how to recover your security deposit.

Eviction process & how long it takes

Self-help eviction is illegal — a landlord cannot change the locks or shut off utilities and must go through the District Court. The sequence:

  • Grace period & notice: for nonpayment, the landlord must wait until rent is 15 days late, then serve a 5-day demand for the rent
  • Complaint filed in District Court; you are served and given a court date
  • Hearing — appear and raise any defenses (the rent was paid, bad conditions, improper notice, retaliation); paying what’s owed often resolves a nonpayment case
  • Judgment and execution: if the landlord wins, the court issues an execution (writ) after a stay, and a constable carries out the removal

From the grace period to removal, a Rhode Island eviction commonly takes about one to two months. Get help from Rhode Island Legal Services (1-800-662-5034), and read how to avoid eviction.

Where to Get Help in Rhode Island

Tenant help & legal aid: Rhode Island Legal Services (1-800-662-5034) and the Center for Justice handle eviction and housing cases.

Source-of-income / discrimination: the Rhode Island Commission for Human Rights ((401) 222-2661) enforces the Fair Housing Practices Act.

Find your local PHA: HUD’s PHA directory or our how to find your PHA guide.

211 helpline: dial 2-1-1 or visit 211ri.org for rental help, shelters, and utility assistance.

HUD fair housing: file at hud.gov/reporthousingdiscrimination or call 1-800-669-9777.
