Utah Housing Resources & Tenant Rights

Waythrough Project · Updated May 2026

Utah’s landlord-tenant rules come from the Fit Premises Act (Utah Code Title 57, Chapter 22) and the state’s eviction (unlawful detainer) statute. There is no rent control and no source-of-income protection — Utah law specifically says refusing to take a Housing Choice Voucher is not discrimination. Utah eviction moves on a 3-day notice and carries a treble-damages risk, so never ignore one. This page explains the statewide framework, the eviction timeline, and where to get help.

Quick numbers to write down:
  • 211 Utah — free, 24/7 — dial 2-1-1 for rental help, shelters, and utilities
  • Utah Legal Services: 1-800-662-4245 · utahlegalservices.org
  • Utah Antidiscrimination & Labor Division (fair housing): 1-800-222-1238
  • Utah Housing Corporation: utahhousingcorp.org
  • HUD fair housing: 1-800-669-9777

Public Housing & Vouchers in Utah

Section 8 Housing Choice Vouchers and public housing in Utah are run by local housing authorities (in cities like Salt Lake City, West Valley City, Provo, and Ogden), and the Utah Housing Corporation supports affordable-housing development statewide. To find the authority that serves you, use HUD’s PHA directory or read how to find your PHA. Each authority keeps its own waitlist, so apply to several. For income-restricted apartments, search HUD’s LIHTC database or read how to find LIHTC housing.

Source of Income & Rent Control in Utah

Utah has no source-of-income protection for renters. Utah’s Fair Housing Act specifically states that a landlord’s refusal to participate in the Housing Choice Voucher program is not a discriminatory housing practice — so a landlord may legally decline Section 8. Federal fair-housing law still bars discrimination based on race, color, national origin, religion, sex, disability, and familial status, and Utah’s law adds sexual orientation and gender identity. See our source-of-income protections guide. Utah also has no rent control, and state law prevents local rent control, so there is no legal cap on rent increases. For a month-to-month tenancy, a landlord generally must give at least 15 days’ written notice before raising the rent or ending the tenancy.

Emergency Rental Assistance in Utah

  • Dial 211 for the current list of rental, deposit, and eviction-prevention funds near you
  • Community Action agencies across the state administer rental and utility assistance — 211 can connect you
  • HEAT (LIHEAP) helps with heating and cooling bills — see utility assistance programs
  • Local nonprofits and the Salt Lake County and Utah housing offices run prevention funds; 211 keeps the current list

Utah Tenant Law: Key Protections at a Glance

Quick Reference: Utah

  • Source-of-income protection: none — Utah law says refusing a voucher is not discrimination
  • Rent control: none — and local rent control is preempted by state law
  • Rent-increase notice (month-to-month): at least 15 days
  • Nonpayment notice: 3 days to pay or quit
  • Habitability: the Fit Premises Act requires safe, sanitary, fit housing; tenant remedies follow written notice
  • Security deposit: no statutory cap; returned within 30 days with an itemized statement (since May 2025, electronic return is allowed with your consent)
  • Eviction risk: losing an unlawful detainer can mean treble (triple) damages for the holdover period — never ignore a notice

Security deposits

Utah does not cap the security deposit, but after you move out the landlord must return it within 30 days, along with an itemized statement of any deductions for unpaid rent or damage beyond normal wear. As of May 2025, a landlord may return your deposit and any prepaid rent electronically if you consent. Document the unit’s condition at move-in and move-out, and read how to recover your security deposit.

Eviction process & how long it takes

Self-help eviction is illegal — a landlord cannot change the locks or shut off utilities and must go through the court. The sequence (unlawful detainer):

  • Notice: for nonpayment, a 3-day notice to pay or quit; a 3-day notice to comply or quit for a lease violation; and a 3-day notice to quit for serious issues like illegal activity
  • Complaint filed in district court; Utah uses an expedited process, and you must answer within the time stated (often just a few days)
  • Hearing — appear and raise any defenses (the rent was paid, improper notice, conditions, retaliation); if you don’t answer, the landlord usually wins by default
  • Order of restitution: if the landlord wins, the court issues an order and a constable or sheriff carries out the removal — and the court can award treble (triple) damages for the time you stayed after the notice

Because of the fast timeline and the treble-damages risk, a Utah eviction can run in as little as two to six weeks — and waiting it out can be expensive. Act immediately: get help from Utah Legal Services (1-800-662-4245), and read how to avoid eviction.

Where to Get Help in Utah

Tenant help & legal aid: Utah Legal Services (1-800-662-4245) handles eviction and housing cases for income-eligible tenants.

Discrimination complaints: the Utah Antidiscrimination and Labor Division (1-800-222-1238) enforces the Utah Fair Housing Act (note that vouchers are not a protected source of income).

Find your local PHA: HUD’s PHA directory or our how to find your PHA guide.

211 helpline: dial 2-1-1 or visit 211utah.org for rental help, shelters, and utility assistance.

HUD fair housing: file at hud.gov/reporthousingdiscrimination or call 1-800-669-9777.
