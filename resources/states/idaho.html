Idaho Housing Resources & Tenant Rights

Waythrough Project · Updated May 2026

Idaho is a landlord-friendly state, and eviction moves quickly: nonpayment starts with a 3-day notice, and the courts use an expedited process. There is no rent control (and state law blocks local rent control) and no statewide source-of-income protection. Knowing the short timelines and your deposit rights matters here. This page covers the statewide framework, the eviction timeline, and where to get help fast.

Quick numbers to write down:
  • 211 Idaho CareLine — free — dial 2-1-1 for rental help, shelters, and utilities
  • Idaho Legal Aid Services: 1-866-345-0106 · idaholegalaid.org
  • Idaho Human Rights Commission (fair housing): 1-888-249-7025
  • Idaho Housing and Finance Association: idahohousing.com
  • HUD fair housing: 1-800-669-9777

Public Housing & Vouchers in Idaho

Section 8 Housing Choice Vouchers and public housing in Idaho are run by local housing authorities and by the Idaho Housing and Finance Association (IHFA), which administers vouchers across much of the state. To find the program that serves you, use HUD’s PHA directory or read how to find your PHA. Each keeps its own waitlist, so apply to several. For income-restricted apartments, search HUD’s LIHTC database or read how to find LIHTC housing.

Source of Income & Rent Control in Idaho

Idaho has no statewide source-of-income protection, which means that outside of a local ordinance a landlord may legally decline to accept a Housing Choice Voucher. Federal fair-housing law still bars discrimination based on race, color, national origin, religion, sex, disability, and familial status, and the Idaho Human Rights Commission enforces those alongside HUD. See our source-of-income protections guide. Idaho also has no rent control, and state law prevents local rent control, so there is no legal cap on how much rent can rise. For a month-to-month tenancy, a landlord generally must give at least 30 days’ written notice before raising the rent or ending the tenancy.

Emergency Rental Assistance in Idaho

  • Dial 211 (Idaho CareLine) for the current list of rental, deposit, and eviction-prevention funds near you
  • Community Action Partnership agencies administer rental and utility assistance across the state
  • LIHEAP helps with heating and cooling bills — see utility assistance programs
  • Local nonprofits and the Salvation Army often run emergency funds; 211 keeps the current list

Idaho Tenant Law: Key Protections at a Glance

Quick Reference: Idaho

  • Source-of-income protection: none statewide — a landlord may decline a voucher unless a local ordinance says otherwise
  • Rent control: none — and local rent control is preempted by state law
  • Rent-increase notice (month-to-month): at least 30 days
  • Nonpayment notice: 3 days to pay or quit
  • Lease-violation notice: 3 days to comply or quit (for most violations)
  • Security deposit: no statutory cap; returned within 21 days (up to 30 if your lease says so) with an itemized statement
  • Self-help eviction: illegal — only a court and sheriff can remove a tenant

Security deposits

Idaho does not cap the security deposit, but after you move out the landlord must return it within 21 days — or up to 30 days if your lease sets that longer period — along with an itemized statement of any deductions for unpaid rent or damage beyond normal wear. Document the unit’s condition at move-in and move-out, and read how to recover your security deposit.

Eviction process & how long it takes

Self-help eviction is illegal — a landlord cannot change the locks or shut off utilities — but Idaho’s eviction process is fast, especially for nonpayment. The sequence:

  • Notice: for nonpayment, a 3-day notice to pay or quit; for most lease violations, a 3-day notice to comply or quit
  • Complaint filed: if you don’t pay or move, the landlord files an eviction (unlawful detainer); for nonpayment, Idaho law sets an expedited hearing, often within about 12 days
  • Hearing — appear and raise any defenses (the rent was paid, improper notice, conditions, retaliation); if you don’t appear, the landlord usually wins by default
  • Judgment and writ of restitution: if the landlord wins, the court issues a writ and a sheriff carries out the removal, often within a few days

From the 3-day notice to removal, an Idaho eviction can run in as little as two to five weeks. Act immediately — get help from Idaho Legal Aid Services (1-866-345-0106), and read how to avoid eviction.

Where to Get Help in Idaho

Tenant help & legal aid: Idaho Legal Aid Services (1-866-345-0106) handles eviction and housing cases, and the Idaho Attorney General publishes a Landlord and Tenant Manual.

Discrimination complaints: the Idaho Human Rights Commission (1-888-249-7025) enforces fair-housing law.

Find your local PHA: HUD’s PHA directory or our how to find your PHA guide.

211 helpline: dial 2-1-1 or visit idahocareline.org for rental help, shelters, and utility assistance.

HUD fair housing: file at hud.gov/reporthousingdiscrimination or call 1-800-669-9777.
