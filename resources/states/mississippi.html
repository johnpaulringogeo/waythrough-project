Mississippi Housing Resources & Tenant Rights

Waythrough Project · Updated May 2026

Mississippi’s landlord-tenant rules come from the Residential Landlord and Tenant Act (Miss. Code Title 89, Chapter 8). There is no rent control and no statewide source-of-income protection, but Mississippi does give tenants an implied warranty of habitability and a limited repair-and-deduct remedy. Nonpayment starts with a 3-day notice, and your deposit must be returned within 45 days. This page explains the statewide framework, the eviction timeline, and where to get help.

Quick numbers to write down:
  • 211 Mississippi — free, 24/7 — dial 2-1-1 for rental help, shelters, and utilities
  • Mississippi Center for Legal Services (south): 1-800-498-1804
  • North Mississippi Rural Legal Services (north): 1-800-959-6889
  • Mississippi Home Corporation: mshomecorp.com
  • HUD fair housing: 1-800-669-9777

Public Housing & Vouchers in Mississippi

Section 8 Housing Choice Vouchers and public housing in Mississippi are run by local housing authorities (in cities like Jackson, Gulfport, Hattiesburg, and Biloxi), and the Mississippi Home Corporation (MHC) supports affordable-housing development statewide. To find the authority that serves you, use HUD’s PHA directory or read how to find your PHA. Each authority keeps its own waitlist, so apply to several. For income-restricted apartments, search HUD’s LIHTC database or read how to find LIHTC housing.

Source of Income & Rent Control in Mississippi

Mississippi has no statewide source-of-income protection, which means that outside of a local ordinance a landlord may legally decline to accept a Housing Choice Voucher. Federal fair-housing law still bars discrimination based on race, color, national origin, religion, sex, disability, and familial status — and because Mississippi has no state fair-housing agency, those complaints go to HUD. See our source-of-income protections guide. Mississippi also has no rent control, and state law prevents local rent control, so there is no legal cap on how much rent can rise. For a month-to-month tenancy, a landlord generally must give at least 30 days’ written notice before raising the rent or ending the tenancy.

Emergency Rental Assistance in Mississippi

  • Dial 211 for the current list of rental, deposit, and eviction-prevention funds near you
  • Community Action agencies across the state administer rental and utility assistance — 211 can connect you
  • LIHEAP helps with heating and cooling bills — see utility assistance programs
  • Local nonprofits, churches, and the Salvation Army often run emergency funds; 211 keeps the current list

Mississippi Tenant Law: Key Protections at a Glance

Quick Reference: Mississippi

  • Source-of-income protection: none statewide — a landlord may decline a voucher unless a local ordinance says otherwise
  • Rent control: none — and local rent control is preempted by state law
  • Rent-increase notice (month-to-month): at least 30 days
  • Nonpayment notice: 3 days to pay or quit
  • Lease-violation notice: 14 days to cure (30 days to terminate if not fixed)
  • Habitability: implied warranty — after written notice and 30 days, you may repair and deduct up to one month’s rent, or terminate
  • Security deposit: no statutory cap; returned within 45 days with an itemized statement

Security deposits & repairs

Mississippi does not cap the security deposit, but after you move out the landlord must return it within 45 days, along with an itemized statement of any deductions for unpaid rent or damage beyond normal wear. Mississippi also gives you a real repair remedy: if the landlord fails to keep the unit in habitable condition, you can give written notice and, if it isn’t fixed within 30 days, repair and deduct up to one month’s rent or end the lease. Document everything and read how to recover your security deposit.

Eviction process & how long it takes

Self-help eviction is illegal — a landlord cannot change the locks or shut off utilities and must go through the court. The sequence:

  • Notice: for nonpayment, a 3-day notice to pay or quit; for a lease violation, a 14-day notice to cure (with termination in 30 days if not fixed)
  • Complaint filed in justice court; you are served and given a hearing date
  • Hearing — appear and raise any defenses (the rent was paid, improper notice, conditions, retaliation); if you do not appear, the landlord usually wins by default
  • Judgment and warrant: if the landlord wins, the court issues a warrant of removal; you generally have a few days to appeal before a sheriff carries it out

From notice to removal, a Mississippi eviction commonly takes about three to five weeks. Get help early from Mississippi Center for Legal Services (1-800-498-1804) or North Mississippi Rural Legal Services (1-800-959-6889), and read how to avoid eviction.

Where to Get Help in Mississippi

Tenant help & legal aid: Mississippi Center for Legal Services (1-800-498-1804) covers the southern counties; North Mississippi Rural Legal Services (1-800-959-6889) covers the north.

Discrimination complaints: Mississippi has no state fair-housing agency, so file with HUD at 1-800-669-9777.

Find your local PHA: HUD’s PHA directory or our how to find your PHA guide.

211 helpline: dial 2-1-1 or visit 211.org for rental help, shelters, and utility assistance.

HUD fair housing: file at hud.gov/reporthousingdiscrimination or call 1-800-669-9777.
