South Carolina Housing Resources & Tenant Rights

Waythrough Project · Updated May 2026

South Carolina’s landlord-tenant rules come from the Residential Landlord and Tenant Act (S.C. Code Title 27, Chapter 40). There is no rent control and no statewide source-of-income protection, and the eviction process — called ejectment — can move quickly once a landlord files. Knowing the 5-day rule and your deposit rights matters here. This page explains the statewide framework, the eviction timeline, and where to get help.

Quick numbers to write down:
  • 211 South Carolina — free, 24/7 — dial 2-1-1 for rental help, shelters, and utilities
  • South Carolina Legal Services: 1-888-346-5592 · sclegal.org

Public Housing & Vouchers in South Carolina

Section 8 Housing Choice Vouchers and public housing in South Carolina are run by local housing authorities (in cities like Columbia, Charleston, and Greenville), and SC State Housing administers statewide voucher and affordable-housing programs. To find the authority that serves you, use HUD’s PHA directory or read how to find your PHA. Each authority keeps its own waitlist, so apply to several. For income-restricted apartments, search HUD’s LIHTC database or read how to find LIHTC housing.

Source of Income & Rent Control in South Carolina

South Carolina has no statewide source-of-income protection, which means that outside of a local ordinance a landlord may legally decline to accept a Housing Choice Voucher. Federal fair-housing law still bars discrimination based on race, color, national origin, religion, sex, disability, and familial status — see our source-of-income protections guide. South Carolina also has no rent control, so there is no legal cap on how much rent can rise. For a month-to-month tenancy, a landlord generally must give at least 30 days’ written notice before raising the rent or ending the tenancy.

Emergency Rental Assistance in South Carolina

  • Dial 211 (United Way Association of South Carolina) for the current list of rental, deposit, and eviction-prevention funds near you
  • Community Action agencies across the state administer rental and utility assistance — 211 can connect you
  • LIHEAP helps with heating and cooling bills — see utility assistance programs
  • Local nonprofits and churches often run emergency funds; 211 keeps the current list

South Carolina Tenant Law: Key Protections at a Glance

Quick Reference: South Carolina

  • Source-of-income protection: none statewide — a landlord may decline a voucher unless a local ordinance says otherwise
  • Rent control: none — no statewide cap on rent increases
  • Rent-increase notice (month-to-month): at least 30 days
  • Nonpayment notice: 5 days to pay or quit (S.C. Code § 27-40-710) — note many leases include this 5-day notice in writing, so no separate notice is needed
  • Lease-violation notice: 14 days to cure
  • Security deposit: no statutory cap; returned within 30 days with an itemized statement of any deductions
  • Self-help eviction: illegal — only a magistrate’s court and a constable or sheriff can carry out an ejectment

Security deposits

South Carolina does not cap the security deposit, but after you move out the landlord must return it within 30 days, along with an itemized written statement of any deductions for unpaid rent or damage beyond normal wear. If a landlord wrongfully withholds it, you may be able to recover up to three times the amount withheld. Document the unit’s condition at move-in and move-out, and read how to recover your security deposit.

Eviction process & how long it takes

Self-help eviction is illegal — a landlord cannot change the locks or shut off utilities and must go through the magistrate’s court. The sequence:

  • Notice: for nonpayment, a 5-day notice to pay or quit — but if your lease states in writing that rent is due on a set day and that you get 5 days, no separate notice is required
  • Rule to Vacate or Show Cause filed in magistrate’s court; you are served and generally have 10 days to respond and request a hearing
  • Hearing — if you request one, appear and raise defenses (the rent was paid, improper notice, conditions, retaliation); if you do not respond, the court can issue a writ without a hearing
  • Writ of ejectment: if the landlord wins, a writ issues and a constable or sheriff carries out the removal

From notice to removal, a South Carolina ejectment commonly takes about three to five weeks — faster if you don’t respond to the rule. Respond in writing and get help from South Carolina Legal Services (1-888-346-5592), and read how to avoid eviction.

Where to Get Help in South Carolina

Tenant help & legal aid: South Carolina Legal Services (1-888-346-5592) handles eviction and housing cases for income-eligible tenants.

Discrimination complaints: the South Carolina Human Affairs Commission (1-800-521-0725) enforces fair-housing law.

Find your local PHA: HUD’s PHA directory or our how to find your PHA guide.

211 helpline: dial 2-1-1 or visit sc211.org for rental help, shelters, and utility assistance.

HUD fair housing: file at hud.gov/reporthousingdiscrimination or call 1-800-669-9777.
