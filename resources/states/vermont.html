Vermont Housing Resources & Tenant Rights

Waythrough Project · Updated May 2026

Vermont’s landlord-tenant rules come from the Residential Rental Agreements Act (9 V.S.A. Chapter 137). Vermont protects renters from discrimination based on the receipt of public assistance — including Section 8 — so a landlord can’t refuse you for using a voucher. Nonpayment starts with a 14-day notice you can cure by paying, deposits must be returned within 14 days, and habitability protections are strong. This page covers the statewide framework, your deposit rights, and how eviction works.

Public Housing & Vouchers in Vermont

Section 8 Housing Choice Vouchers and public housing in Vermont are run by the Vermont State Housing Authority (VSHA) and local authorities (such as the Burlington Housing Authority). To find the authority that serves you, use HUD’s PHA directory or read how to find your PHA. Each authority keeps its own waitlist, so apply to several. For income-restricted apartments, search HUD’s LIHTC database or read how to find LIHTC housing. Because Vermont protects receipt of public assistance (below), a landlord cannot refuse you for using a voucher.

Source of Income & Public Assistance Protection

Vermont’s Fair Housing and Public Accommodations Act (9 V.S.A. § 4503) lists the receipt of public assistance — including a Section 8 Housing Choice Voucher — among its protected categories, so it is illegal for a landlord to refuse you, or treat you differently, because you pay with a voucher or other assistance. Complaints can go to the CVOEO Fair Housing Project (1-800-540-3895) or the Vermont Human Rights Commission. See our source-of-income protections guide.

Rent Control & Just Cause

Vermont has no statewide rent control and no statewide just-cause eviction standard, so for a month-to-month tenancy a landlord generally must give long written notice of an increase or termination. The city of Burlington has adopted a local just-cause eviction ordinance for many properties, limiting the reasons a landlord can end a tenancy; Burlington and Barre also cap the security deposit at one month. Check your city’s rules, and call 211 if you’re unsure.

Emergency Rental Assistance in Vermont

  • Dial 211 for the current list of rental, deposit, and eviction-prevention funds near you
  • Economic Services Division (Vermont Department for Children and Families) administers rental and emergency assistance
  • LIHEAP / Seasonal Fuel helps with heating bills — see utility assistance programs
  • Community Action agencies administer local prevention funds; 211 keeps the current list

Vermont Tenant Law: Key Protections at a Glance

Quick Reference: Vermont

  • Source-of-income protection: yes — receipt of public assistance (incl. vouchers) is protected under 9 V.S.A. § 4503
  • Rent control: none statewide (Burlington has just-cause; Burlington & Barre cap deposits at one month)
  • Nonpayment notice: 14 days, and you can stop the eviction by paying through the end of the rental period
  • Security deposit: no statewide cap; returned within 14 days with an itemized statement
  • Habitability: strong implied warranty — repair-and-deduct and rent-withholding (into escrow) remedies after notice
  • Self-help eviction: illegal — only the Superior Court and a sheriff can carry out an eviction

Security deposits

Vermont does not cap the security deposit statewide (Burlington and Barre cap it at one month). After you move out, the landlord must return the deposit within 14 days, along with an itemized statement of any deductions for unpaid rent or damage beyond normal wear — and missing that deadline can mean the landlord forfeits the right to keep any of it. Document the unit’s condition at move-in and move-out, and read how to recover your security deposit.

Eviction process & how long it takes

Self-help eviction is illegal — a landlord cannot change the locks or shut off utilities and must go through the Superior Court. The sequence:

  • Notice: for nonpayment, a 14-day notice — and you can stop the eviction by paying everything owed through the end of the rental period; other terminations require their own (often longer) notice
  • Complaint filed in Superior Court; you are served and can file an Answer raising defenses
  • Hearing — Vermont recognizes a strong warranty-of-habitability defense, so bad conditions can reduce or offset what you owe
  • Judgment and writ of possession: if the landlord wins, a writ issues and a sheriff carries out the removal

Because of the notice, the court process, and habitability defenses, a Vermont eviction commonly takes one to three months or longer. Get help from Vermont Legal Aid (1-800-889-2047), and read how to avoid eviction.

Where to Get Help in Vermont

Tenant help & legal aid: Vermont Legal Aid and Legal Services Vermont (1-800-889-2047) handle eviction and housing cases and run VTLawHelp.org.

Source-of-income / discrimination: the CVOEO Fair Housing Project (1-800-540-3895) and the Vermont Human Rights Commission handle housing discrimination.

Find your local PHA: HUD’s PHA directory or our how to find your PHA guide.

211 helpline: dial 2-1-1 or visit vermont211.org for rental help, shelters, and utility assistance.
