Iowa Housing Resources & Tenant Rights

Waythrough Project · Updated May 2026

Iowa’s landlord-tenant rules come from the Uniform Residential Landlord and Tenant Law (Iowa Code Chapter 562A). There is no rent control — and state law bars cities from enacting it — and no statewide source-of-income protection. In fact, a 2021 law went further and barred local governments from requiring landlords to accept Housing Choice Vouchers. On the tenant’s side, Iowa does cap security deposits and late fees. This page explains the statewide framework, the eviction timeline, and where to get help.

Quick numbers to write down:

Public Housing & Vouchers in Iowa

Section 8 Housing Choice Vouchers and public housing in Iowa are run by local housing authorities (in cities like Des Moines, Cedar Rapids, Davenport, and Iowa City), and the Iowa Finance Authority (IFA) supports affordable-housing development statewide. To find the authority that serves you, use HUD’s PHA directory or read how to find your PHA. Each authority keeps its own waitlist, so apply to several. For income-restricted apartments, search HUD’s LIHTC database or read how to find LIHTC housing.

Source of Income & Rent Control in Iowa

Iowa has no statewide source-of-income protection, so a landlord may legally decline to accept a Housing Choice Voucher. A 2021 law went a step further and prohibited Iowa cities and counties from requiring landlords to accept vouchers — voiding local ordinances that had been adopted in places like Des Moines, Iowa City, and Marion. Federal fair-housing law still bars discrimination based on race, color, national origin, religion, sex, disability, and familial status — see our source-of-income protections guide. Iowa also has no rent control, and state law prevents local rent control, so there is no legal cap on rent increases. For a month-to-month tenancy, a landlord generally must give at least 30 days’ written notice before raising the rent.

Iowa Tenant Law: Key Protections at a Glance

Quick Reference: Iowa

  • Source-of-income protection: none — and cities are barred from requiring landlords to accept vouchers (2021 law)
  • Rent control: none — and local rent control is preempted by state law
  • Rent-increase notice (month-to-month): at least 30 days
  • Nonpayment notice: 3 days to pay or quit (Iowa Code § 562A.27)
  • Lease-violation notice: 7 days to cure
  • Security deposit: capped at 2 months’ rent; returned within 30 days with an itemized statement
  • Late fees: capped by statute — about $12/day or $60/month for rent up to $700, and $20/day or $100/month above that

Security deposits & late fees

An Iowa landlord may not collect more than two months’ rent as a security deposit, and must return it within 30 days after you move out and provide a forwarding address, along with an itemized statement of any deductions. Iowa also caps late fees: for rent of $700 or less a month, no more than $12 a day or $60 a month; for rent above $700, no more than $20 a day or $100 a month. See how to recover your security deposit.

Eviction process & how long it takes

Self-help eviction is illegal — a landlord cannot change the locks or shut off utilities and must go through the court. The sequence (forcible entry and detainer, or FED):

  • Notice: for nonpayment, a 3-day notice to pay or quit; for a lease violation, a 7-day notice to cure
  • FED petition filed in district court; a hearing is set quickly — often within a couple of weeks
  • Hearing — appear and raise any defenses (the rent was paid, improper notice, conditions, retaliation); if you do not appear, the landlord usually wins by default
  • Judgment and writ: if the landlord wins, the court issues a writ of removal and a sheriff carries it out (often after about 3 days)

Because the FED process is fast, an Iowa eviction can run in as little as two to four weeks. Act quickly — get help from Iowa Legal Aid (1-800-532-1275), and read how to avoid eviction.

Where to Get Help in Iowa

Tenant help & legal aid: Iowa Legal Aid (1-800-532-1275) handles eviction and housing cases for income-eligible tenants and publishes plain-language guides.

Discrimination complaints: the Iowa Civil Rights Commission (1-800-457-4416) enforces fair-housing law.

Find your local PHA: HUD’s PHA directory or our how to find your PHA guide.

211 helpline: dial 2-1-1 or visit 211iowa.org for rental help, shelters, and utility assistance.

HUD fair housing: file at hud.gov/reporthousingdiscrimination or call 1-800-669-9777.
