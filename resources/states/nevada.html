Nevada Housing Resources & Tenant Rights

Waythrough Project · Updated May 2026

Nevada’s rental rules are in NRS Chapter 118A, and the state has one feature every renter must understand: the summary eviction process puts the burden on you. After a 7-day pay-or-quit notice, if you do not file a written response (an affidavit) with the court in time, the landlord can get a lockout order without ever filing a lawsuit. There is no rent control, security deposits can be as high as three months’ rent, and there is no statewide source-of-income protection. The Nevada Housing Division finances affordable housing. This page covers the statewide rules, the eviction timeline, and links to every Nevada city we cover.

Quick numbers to write down:
  • 211 Nevada — free, 24/7 — for any housing emergency anywhere in Nevada
  • Legal Aid Center of Southern Nevada: (702) 386-1070 · Nevada Legal Services (statewide)
  • Nevada Housing Division: housing.nv.gov

Public Housing & Vouchers in Nevada

Section 8 Housing Choice Vouchers and public housing are run by local authorities — the Southern Nevada Regional Housing Authority (Las Vegas/Clark County), the Reno Housing Authority, and the Nevada Rural Housing Authority. The Nevada Housing Division finances affordable apartments and allocates Low-Income Housing Tax Credits — search HUD’s LIHTC database or read how to find LIHTC housing.

Apply to several authorities at once. Use HUD’s PHA directory or our how to find your PHA and how to apply for Section 8 guides. City waitlist status is on the city pages below.

Nevada’s Summary Eviction: Why You Must Respond

This is the most important thing to know in Nevada. Under NRS 40.253, after a landlord serves a 7-day notice to pay or quit for nonpayment, the burden flips to the tenant: you must file a written response (a tenant’s affidavit) with the justice court stating your defense (for example, that the rent was paid or not owed). If you do not file in time, the court can issue an eviction order and the constable can lock you out — without the landlord ever filing a formal complaint. So in Nevada, doing nothing means you lose. If you receive a notice, file your affidavit right away and bring proof of payment.

Rent Control & Source of Income

There is no rent control in Nevada — no statewide cap and no city has one, so a landlord can raise the rent at renewal with proper notice. Nevada also has no statewide source-of-income protection, so a landlord may decline a Housing Choice Voucher; check whether your city has any local rule, and otherwise look for landlords who already participate. Read our source-of-income protections guide.

Emergency Rental Assistance in Nevada

  • Dial 211 to reach local emergency rental and prevention funds and your regional Coordinated Entry for shelter and rapid re-housing
  • The Nevada Energy Assistance Program (LIHEAP) through the Division of Welfare and Supportive Services helps with utility bills — see utility assistance programs
  • The pandemic-era CHAP rental assistance program has ended; check 211 and your county for what is currently open
  • The Salvation Army, Catholic Charities, and St. Vincent de Paul provide one-time help across the state

Nevada Tenant Law: Key Protections at a Glance

Quick Reference: Nevada (NV)

  • Source-of-income protection: none statewide
  • Rent control: none statewide and in no city
  • Security deposit limit: 3 months’ rent (NRS 118A.242)
  • Deposit return deadline: 30 days, itemized
  • Eviction notice (nonpayment): 7-day pay-or-quit (NRS 40.253)
  • Summary eviction: the tenant must file a court affidavit to contest, or risk a lockout with no lawsuit filed
  • Notice to end month-to-month: 30 days (NRS 40.251)
  • Self-help eviction: illegal — an unlawful lockout can cost the landlord damages (NRS 118A.390)

Security deposits

Under NRS 118A.242, a deposit (including any non-refundable fees and pet deposits combined) cannot exceed three months’ rent. After you move out, the landlord must return the deposit with an itemized statement of any deductions within 30 days. If the landlord wrongfully withholds it, you can sue for the amount plus damages. See how to recover your security deposit.

Eviction process & how long it takes

Self-help eviction — lockouts, removing belongings, shutting off utilities — is illegal (NRS 118A.390). Nevada’s summary process moves fast:

  • 7-day pay-or-quit notice for nonpayment (other grounds have their own notice periods)
  • Tenant’s affidavit: to fight it, you must file a written response with the justice court before the deadline. This is the step that protects you
  • Court review: if you file, a hearing is set; if you do not, the court can issue an eviction order on the landlord’s filing alone
  • Lockout: the constable carries out the order, often within a day or two of it being issued

Because of the summary process, a Nevada eviction can finish in as little as two to three weeks — and faster if you do not respond. File your affidavit and get help the same day from the Legal Aid Center of Southern Nevada or Nevada Legal Services, and read how to avoid eviction.

Where to Get Help in Nevada

Free legal aid: the Legal Aid Center of Southern Nevada ((702) 386-1070) and Nevada Legal Services (statewide) handle eviction defense and deposits.

State housing agency: the Nevada Housing Division for LIHTC and affordable-housing programs.

Find your local PHA: HUD’s PHA directory or our how to find your PHA guide.

211 helpline: dial 2-1-1 or visit 211.org for rental help, shelters, and utility assistance.

HUD fair housing: file at hud.gov/reporthousingdiscrimination or call 1-800-669-9777.
