Kentucky Housing Resources & Tenant Rights

Waythrough Project · Updated May 2026

Kentucky is unusual: its main tenant-protection law, the Uniform Residential Landlord and Tenant Act (URLTA), applies only in the cities and counties that have adopted it — including Louisville/Jefferson County and Lexington/Fayette County and about a dozen others. Where it applies, you get clear notice periods, deposit rules, and habitability protections; elsewhere, only general common law applies. There is no rent control (KRS 65.875), and in 2024 the state preempted local source-of-income protections (HB 18), so vouchers can be refused statewide. The Kentucky Housing Corporation (KHC) finances affordable housing. This page covers the statewide rules, what changes where URLTA applies, the eviction timeline, and links to every Kentucky city we cover.

Quick numbers to write down:

  • Legal Aid Society (Louisville): 1-800-292-1862 · kyjustice.org

Public Housing & Vouchers in Kentucky

Section 8 Housing Choice Vouchers and public housing are run by local authorities — the Louisville Metro Housing Authority, the Lexington-Fayette Urban County Housing Authority, and others. The Kentucky Housing Corporation (KHC) administers vouchers across much of the state and allocates Low-Income Housing Tax Credits — search HUD’s LIHTC database or read how to find LIHTC housing.

Apply to several authorities at once. Use HUD’s PHA directory or our how to find your PHA and how to apply for Section 8 guides. City waitlist status is on the city pages below.

Does URLTA Apply to You?

This is the first thing to check in Kentucky. The URLTA (KRS 383.500–383.715) only governs your tenancy if your city or county adopted it. Adopting jurisdictions include Louisville/Jefferson County, Lexington/Fayette County, Covington, Florence, Newport, Georgetown, Shively, Oldham County, and Pulaski County, among others. Where URLTA applies, you get:

  • A 7-day notice to pay or quit for nonpayment, and a notice with a chance to cure (about 14–15 days) for many lease violations
  • Security-deposit protections (escrow account, itemized accounting) under KRS 383.580
  • An implied warranty of habitability and protections against retaliation

If your area has not adopted URLTA, your lease and general Kentucky common law control, with fewer built-in protections — so read your lease carefully and confirm your jurisdiction’s status.

Source of Income & Rent Control

Kentucky has no statewide source-of-income protection, and in 2024 the legislature passed HB 18, which preempts local source-of-income ordinances (KRS 65.874) — barring cities from requiring landlords to accept federal housing assistance. The result is that a landlord can decline a Housing Choice Voucher across the state. Part of using a voucher in Kentucky is finding landlords who already participate — ask your PHA for its list and read our source-of-income protections guide.

There is also no rent control: KRS 65.875 reserves rent regulation to the General Assembly, so no city or county can cap rents.

Emergency Rental Assistance in Kentucky

  • Dial 211 to reach local emergency rental and prevention funds and your regional Coordinated Entry for shelter and rapid re-housing
  • Community Action Kentucky agencies administer crisis help and the LIHEAP energy-assistance program — see utility assistance programs
  • The pandemic-era Healthy at Home Eviction Relief Fund has ended; check 211 and KHC for what is currently open
  • The Salvation Army, Catholic Charities, and St. Vincent de Paul provide one-time help across the state

Kentucky Tenant Law: Key Protections at a Glance

Quick Reference: Kentucky (KY)

  • URLTA: applies only in adopting jurisdictions (Louisville, Lexington, and others)
  • Source-of-income protection: none — a 2024 law (HB 18) preempts local ordinances; vouchers can be refused
  • Rent control: none — preempted statewide (KRS 65.875)
  • Security deposit: no cap; in URLTA areas, must be held in a separate account with itemized accounting (KRS 383.580)
  • Eviction notice (nonpayment, URLTA areas): 7-day pay-or-quit
  • Lease violation (URLTA areas): notice with a chance to cure (about 14–15 days)
  • Warranty of habitability: yes in URLTA areas; limited elsewhere
  • Self-help eviction: illegal — only the sheriff can remove a tenant

Security deposits

Kentucky sets no cap on the deposit amount. In URLTA jurisdictions, KRS 383.580 requires the landlord to hold your deposit in a separate account at an insured Kentucky institution, to have listed any existing damage when you moved in, and to give an itemized accounting of deductions before keeping any of it. If the landlord did not follow these steps, that strengthens your claim to a full refund. See how to recover your security deposit.

Eviction (forcible detainer) process & timeline

Self-help eviction is illegal; the landlord must go to court and only the sheriff can remove you. Kentucky’s process (a “forcible detainer”) is:

  • Written notice — in URLTA areas, a 7-day pay-or-quit notice for nonpayment, or a cure notice for lease violations
  • Forcible detainer complaint filed in district court; you are served and given a court date, often within a week or two
  • Hearing — appear and raise defenses; if you do not, the court can rule for the landlord
  • Judgment and writ: if the landlord wins, you typically have about 7 days before a writ of possession issues and the sheriff carries out the eviction

Because hearings come quickly, an uncontested Kentucky eviction can run in as little as two to four weeks. Appear at your hearing with your lease, receipts, and photos. Get help at kyjustice.org and read how to avoid eviction.

Where to Get Help in Kentucky

Free legal aid: the Legal Aid Society (Louisville, 1-800-292-1862), Legal Aid of the Bluegrass (Lexington/northern KY), and Kentucky Legal Aid (south-central/western KY) handle eviction defense; start at kyjustice.org.

State housing agency: Kentucky Housing Corporation for vouchers, LIHTC, and programs.

Find your local PHA: HUD’s PHA directory or our how to find your PHA guide.

211 helpline: dial 2-1-1 or visit 211.org for rental help, shelters, and utility assistance.

HUD fair housing: file at hud.gov/reporthousingdiscrimination or call 1-800-669-9777.
