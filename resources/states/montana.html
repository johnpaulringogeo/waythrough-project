Montana Housing Resources & Tenant Rights

Waythrough Project · Updated May 2026

Montana’s landlord-tenant rules come from the Residential Landlord and Tenant Act of 1977 (Montana Code Title 70, Chapter 24). There is no rent control and no statewide source-of-income protection, so knowing the notice periods and your deposit rights matters. Nonpayment starts with a 3-day notice, and your deposit must come back within 10 to 30 days. This page covers the statewide framework, the eviction timeline, and where to get help.

Quick numbers to write down:
  • 211 Montana — free, 24/7 — dial 2-1-1 for rental help, shelters, and utilities
  • Montana Legal Services Association: 1-800-666-6899 · mtlsa.org
  • Montana Human Rights Bureau (fair housing): 1-800-542-0807
  • Montana Housing: housing.mt.gov
  • HUD fair housing: 1-800-669-9777

Public Housing & Vouchers in Montana

Section 8 Housing Choice Vouchers and public housing in Montana are run by Montana Housing (in the Department of Commerce) and local authorities (in cities like Billings, Missoula, Great Falls, and Bozeman). To find the authority that serves you, use HUD’s PHA directory or read how to find your PHA. Each authority keeps its own waitlist, so apply to several. For income-restricted apartments, search HUD’s LIHTC database or read how to find LIHTC housing.

Source of Income & Rent Control in Montana

Montana has no statewide source-of-income protection, which means that outside of a local ordinance a landlord may legally decline to accept a Housing Choice Voucher. Federal fair-housing law still bars discrimination based on race, color, national origin, religion, sex, disability, and familial status, and the Montana Human Rights Bureau enforces those alongside HUD. See our source-of-income protections guide. Montana also has no rent control, and state law prevents local rent control, so there is no legal cap on how much rent can rise. For a month-to-month tenancy, a landlord generally must give at least 30 days’ written notice before raising the rent or ending the tenancy.

Emergency Rental Assistance in Montana

  • Dial 211 for the current list of rental, deposit, and eviction-prevention funds near you
  • Human Resource Development Councils (HRDCs) across the state administer rental and utility assistance
  • LIEAP helps with heating bills, which matter a lot in Montana — see utility assistance programs
  • Local nonprofits and tribal programs run emergency funds; 211 keeps the current list

Montana Tenant Law: Key Protections at a Glance

Quick Reference: Montana

  • Source-of-income protection: none statewide — a landlord may decline a voucher unless a local ordinance says otherwise
  • Rent control: none — and local rent control is preempted by state law
  • Rent-increase notice (month-to-month): at least 30 days
  • Nonpayment notice: 3 days to pay or quit (MCA § 70-24-422)
  • Lease-violation notice: generally 14 days to cure (3 days for damage or unauthorized pets/people)
  • Security deposit: no statutory cap; returned within 30 days (or 10 days if there are no deductions) with an itemized statement
  • Self-help eviction: illegal — only a court and a sheriff can remove a tenant

Security deposits

Montana does not cap the security deposit, but the return rules are clear: after you move out, the landlord must return it within 30 days, or within 10 days if no deductions are taken, along with an itemized statement of any deductions for unpaid rent, cleaning, or damage beyond normal wear. Document the unit’s condition at move-in and move-out, and read how to recover your security deposit.

Eviction process & how long it takes

Self-help eviction is illegal — a landlord cannot change the locks or shut off utilities and must go through the court. The sequence:

  • Notice: for nonpayment, a 3-day notice to pay or quit; for many lease violations, a 14-day notice to cure (3 days for damage or unauthorized occupants/pets)
  • Complaint filed in Justice or District Court; you are served and given a hearing date
  • Hearing — appear and raise any defenses (the rent was paid, improper notice, conditions, retaliation); if you do not appear, the landlord usually wins by default
  • Judgment and writ of possession: if the landlord wins, the court issues a writ and a sheriff carries out the removal

From notice to removal, a Montana eviction commonly takes about three to five weeks. Get help early from Montana Legal Services Association (1-800-666-6899), and read how to avoid eviction.

Where to Get Help in Montana

Tenant help & legal aid: Montana Legal Services Association (1-800-666-6899) handles eviction and housing cases for income-eligible tenants.

Discrimination complaints: the Montana Human Rights Bureau (1-800-542-0807) enforces fair-housing law.

Find your local PHA: HUD’s PHA directory or our how to find your PHA guide.

211 helpline: dial 2-1-1 or visit montana211.org for rental help, shelters, and utility assistance.

HUD fair housing: file at hud.gov/reporthousingdiscrimination or call 1-800-669-9777.
