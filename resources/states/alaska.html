Alaska Housing Resources & Tenant Rights

Waythrough Project · Updated May 2026

Alaska’s landlord-tenant rules come from the Uniform Residential Landlord and Tenant Act (Alaska Statutes 34.03). There is no rent control and no statewide source-of-income protection, so knowing the notice periods and your deposit rights matters. Nonpayment starts with a 7-day notice, and your deposit is capped at two months’ rent and due back within 14 days. This page covers the statewide framework, the eviction timeline, and where to get help.

Quick numbers to write down:
  • 211 Alaska (United Way) — free — dial 2-1-1 for rental help, shelters, and utilities
  • Alaska Legal Services Corporation: 1-888-478-2572 · alsc-law.org
  • Alaska State Commission for Human Rights (fair housing): 1-800-478-4692
  • Alaska Housing Finance Corporation: ahfc.us
  • HUD fair housing: 1-800-669-9777

Public Housing & Vouchers in Alaska

Section 8 Housing Choice Vouchers and public housing in Alaska are run by the Alaska Housing Finance Corporation (AHFC) and regional housing authorities (such as Cook Inlet Housing Authority and the Tlingit-Haida Regional Housing Authority). To find the program that serves you, use HUD’s PHA directory or read how to find your PHA. Each keeps its own waitlist, so apply to several. For income-restricted apartments, search HUD’s LIHTC database or read how to find LIHTC housing.

Source of Income & Rent Control in Alaska

Alaska has no statewide source-of-income protection, which means that outside of a local ordinance a landlord may legally decline to accept a Housing Choice Voucher. Federal fair-housing law still bars discrimination based on race, color, national origin, religion, sex, disability, and familial status, and the Alaska State Commission for Human Rights enforces those alongside HUD. See our source-of-income protections guide. Alaska also has no rent control, so there is no legal cap on how much rent can rise. For a month-to-month tenancy, a landlord generally must give at least 30 days’ written notice before raising the rent or ending the tenancy.

Emergency Rental Assistance in Alaska

  • Dial 211 (United Way of Alaska) for the current list of rental, deposit, and eviction-prevention funds near you
  • AHFC and regional nonprofits administer rental and homelessness-prevention programs
  • Heating Assistance Program / LIHEAP helps with energy bills, which matter a lot in Alaska — see utility assistance programs
  • Tribal and regional organizations run housing programs; 211 keeps the current list

Alaska Tenant Law: Key Protections at a Glance

Quick Reference: Alaska

  • Source-of-income protection: none statewide — a landlord may decline a voucher unless a local ordinance says otherwise
  • Rent control: none — no statewide cap on rent increases
  • Rent-increase notice (month-to-month): at least 30 days
  • Nonpayment notice: 7 days to pay or quit (AS 34.03.220)
  • Security deposit: capped at two months’ rent (no cap if rent is over $2,000 a month); returned within 14 days with an itemized statement
  • Self-help eviction: illegal — only a court and a peace officer can remove a tenant

Security deposits

An Alaska landlord may not collect more than two months’ rent as a security deposit, unless the rent is more than $2,000 a month (then the cap doesn’t apply). When you give proper notice and move out, the landlord must return the deposit with an itemized statement within 14 days (the period can be 30 days if you leave without giving the required notice). Document the unit’s condition at move-in and move-out, and read how to recover your security deposit.

Eviction process & how long it takes

Self-help eviction is illegal — a landlord cannot change the locks or shut off utilities and must go through the court. The sequence (forcible entry and detainer, or FED):

  • Notice: for nonpayment, a 7-day notice to pay or quit; for many lease violations, a 10-day notice to cure
  • FED complaint filed in District Court; you are served and a first hearing is set quickly
  • Hearing — appear and raise any defenses (the rent was paid, improper notice, conditions, retaliation); if you do not appear, the landlord usually wins by default
  • Judgment and writ of assistance: if the landlord wins, the court issues a writ and a peace officer carries out the removal

From notice to removal, an Alaska eviction commonly takes about two to four weeks. Act quickly — get help from Alaska Legal Services Corporation (1-888-478-2572), and read how to avoid eviction.

Other Housing Programs in Alaska

  • HUD-VASH (veterans) — a voucher paired with VA case management; see how to apply for HUD-VASH
  • Permanent Supportive Housing & rapid re-housing — access through Coordinated Entry by calling 211
  • Tribal housing — regional housing authorities serve many communities; 211 and AHFC can point you to the right one
  • Eviction prevention — our eviction prevention hub explains what to do before your court date

Where to Get Help in Alaska

Tenant help & legal aid: Alaska Legal Services Corporation (1-888-478-2572) handles eviction and housing cases statewide, and the Alaska Department of Law publishes a Landlord & Tenant Act guide.

Discrimination complaints: the Alaska State Commission for Human Rights (1-800-478-4692) enforces fair-housing law.

Find your local PHA: HUD’s PHA directory or our how to find your PHA guide.

211 helpline: dial 2-1-1 or visit alaska211.org for rental help, shelters, and utility assistance.

HUD fair housing: file at hud.gov/reporthousingdiscrimination or call 1-800-669-9777.
