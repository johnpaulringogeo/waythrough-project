Nebraska Housing Resources & Tenant Rights

Waythrough Project · Updated May 2026

Nebraska’s landlord-tenant rules come from the Uniform Residential Landlord and Tenant Act (Neb. Rev. Stat. § 76-1401 and following). There is no rent control and no statewide source-of-income protection, so knowing the notice periods and your deposit rights matters. Nonpayment of rent starts with a 7-day notice, and your security deposit is capped at one month’s rent. This page explains the statewide framework, the eviction timeline, and where to get help. Public Housing & Vouchers in Nebraska

Section 8 Housing Choice Vouchers and public housing in Nebraska are run by local housing authorities (such as those in Omaha and Lincoln), and the Nebraska Investment Finance Authority (NIFA) supports affordable-housing development statewide. To find the authority that serves you, use HUD’s PHA directory or read how to find your PHA. Each authority keeps its own waitlist, so apply to several. For income-restricted apartments, search HUD’s LIHTC database or read how to find LIHTC housing.

Source of Income & Rent Control in Nebraska

Nebraska has no statewide source-of-income protection, which means that outside of a local ordinance a landlord may legally decline to accept a Housing Choice Voucher. (A few communities have looked at local protections — Lincoln considered a measure — but there is no statewide rule.) Federal fair-housing law still bars discrimination based on race, color, national origin, religion, sex, disability, and familial status — see our source-of-income protections guide. Nebraska also has no rent control, so there is no legal cap on how much rent can rise. For a month-to-month tenancy, a landlord generally must give at least 30 days’ written notice before raising the rent or ending the tenancy.

Nebraska Tenant Law: Key Protections at a Glance

Quick Reference: Nebraska

  • Source-of-income protection: none statewide — a landlord may decline a voucher unless a local ordinance says otherwise
  • Rent control: none — no statewide cap on rent increases
  • Rent-increase notice (month-to-month): at least 30 days
  • Security deposit: capped at one month’s rent (plus a pet deposit of up to one-quarter month); returned within 14 days with an itemized statement
  • Nonpayment notice: 7 days to pay or the lease can be terminated (Neb. Rev. Stat. § 76-1431)
  • Lease-violation notice: 14 days to cure, with termination in 30 days if not fixed
  • Self-help eviction: illegal — the landlord must use the courts to remove a tenant

Security deposits

A Nebraska landlord may not collect more than one month’s rent as a security deposit (a separate pet deposit of up to one-quarter of a month’s rent is allowed). After you move out and provide a forwarding address, the landlord must return the deposit within 14 days, along with an itemized statement of any deductions for unpaid rent or damage beyond normal wear. Document the unit’s condition at move-in and move-out, and read how to recover your security deposit.

Eviction process & how long it takes

Self-help eviction is illegal — a landlord cannot change the locks or shut off utilities and must go through the court. The sequence under the Act:

  • Notice: for nonpayment, a 7-day notice to pay or the rental agreement terminates; for a lease violation, a 14-day notice to cure (with termination in 30 days if not fixed)
  • Complaint filed in county or district court for restitution of the premises; you are served and given a hearing date
  • Hearing — appear and raise any defenses (improper notice, the rent was paid, conditions, retaliation); if you do not appear, the landlord usually wins by default
  • Judgment and writ: if the landlord wins, the court issues a writ of restitution and a sheriff carries out the removal

From notice to removal, a Nebraska eviction commonly takes a few weeks to about two months. Get help early from Legal Aid of Nebraska (1-877-250-2016), and read how to avoid eviction.

Where to Get Help in Nebraska

Tenant help & legal aid: Legal Aid of Nebraska (1-877-250-2016) handles eviction and housing cases and publishes a tenant handbook.

Discrimination complaints: the Nebraska Equal Opportunity Commission (1-800-642-6112) enforces fair-housing law.

Find your local PHA: HUD’s PHA directory or our how to find your PHA guide.

211 helpline: dial 2-1-1 or visit 211.org for rental help, shelters, and utility assistance.

HUD fair housing: file at hud.gov/reporthousingdiscrimination or call 1-800-669-9777.
