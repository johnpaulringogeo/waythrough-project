Housing Resources for People with Disabilities

Disability doesn't disqualify you—it opens access to specialized housing programs and legal protections.

Section 811: Supportive Housing for Persons with Disabilities

Section 811 is a dedicated program that provides rental assistance specifically for very low-income persons with disabilities. Unlike standard Section 8, Section 811 is designed to help people with disabilities live independently in the community with supportive services tailored to their needs.

The program provides a voucher that covers most or all of your rent, paired with access to supportive services such as case management, job training, independent living skills, or other assistance based on your disability and goals. This can include mental health services, substance abuse treatment, physical therapy, or adaptive equipment.

Section 811 Eligibility

To qualify for Section 811, you must:

  • Be at least 18 years old
  • Have a disability (physical, psychiatric, developmental, or intellectual)
  • Have a very low income (generally 30-50% of area median income)
  • Meet other local program requirements

The program defines disability broadly. If you have a documented condition that substantially limits one or more major life activities, you likely qualify. This includes physical disabilities, learning disabilities, mental health conditions, cognitive disabilities, and more.

How to Apply for Section 811

Section 811 is administered through Public Housing Authorities (PHAs) in your area. Some PHAs manage it directly; others contract with nonprofits. Here's how to find and apply:

  • Contact your local PHA and ask specifically about Section 811 Supportive Housing for Persons with Disabilities
  • Ask about current applications, waitlists, and timelines
  • Be prepared to provide proof of disability (medical documentation, Social Security disability determination, etc.)
  • Complete the application and let them know about your housing and service needs
  • If accepted, you'll be assigned to a case manager who helps coordinate services and housing

Note: Like many housing programs, Section 811 sometimes has waiting lists. Apply anyway—funding does become available, and there are often special initiatives to serve particular populations.

Reasonable Accommodations: Your Right to Accessible Housing

Under the Fair Housing Act and the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), landlords, Public Housing Authorities, and housing programs must make "reasonable accommodations" to ensure people with disabilities have equal access to housing. A reasonable accommodation is a change or adjustment to a rule, policy, practice, or service that allows you to use and enjoy your home.

This is a critical protection that many people with disabilities don't know about. You don't have to accept a landlord's refusal of a service animal, an inaccessible unit, or a policy that prevents you from making necessary modifications. You have the right to request accommodations, and landlords have a legal obligation to consider them.

Types of Reasonable Accommodations

Reasonable accommodations can include:

  • Assistive Animals: Service dogs, emotional support animals, or psychiatric service animals (landlords cannot charge pet fees)
  • Accessibility Modifications: Ramps, grab bars, accessible parking spaces, widened doorways, or accessible bathrooms
  • Policy Exceptions: Allowing frequent medical appointments that might technically violate a lease clause, extra household members for caregivers, or adapted lease terms
  • Communication Accommodations: Large print leases, accessible communication formats, extra time to review documents
  • Flexibility with Standards: Modified application processes if you have difficulty filling out standard forms, flexibility on credit checks if you have medical debt

How to Request a Reasonable Accommodation

You can request a reasonable accommodation from any housing program (Section 8, public housing, private landlords, etc.) if the program or landlord is covered by the Fair Housing Act. Here's the process, and for a detailed step-by-step guide, see Request a Reasonable Accommodation:

  1. Make the Request in Writing: Send a letter or email to your landlord, PHA, or housing program. Email is fine and creates a paper trail.
  2. Be Clear About Your Disability and Limitation: Explain how your disability affects your ability to use and enjoy your home. You don't need to disclose your diagnosis, but you do need to explain the functional limitation. Example: "I have a mobility disability that makes it difficult to climb stairs. I'm requesting a ground-floor unit."
  3. Connect to Your Request: Explain the specific accommodation you need. Example: "A service animal would help me manage anxiety in public spaces, including housing."
  4. Provide Supporting Documentation: Include a letter from a healthcare provider (doctor, therapist, psychiatrist, case manager) confirming your disability and stating that the accommodation is necessary. This is important—landlords can request documentation.
  5. Follow Up: If you don't get a response in a few days, call and ask about the status. Keep records of all communications.

When a Landlord Says No

If a landlord denies your reasonable accommodation request, they must provide a legitimate reason unrelated to your disability. Common valid reasons include:

  • The accommodation would fundamentally alter the program or create an undue financial hardship
  • The accommodation would pose a direct threat to safety (narrow exception, rarely applies)

Invalid reasons include cost to the landlord, inconvenience, or general discomfort with disabilities. If you believe your request was unfairly denied, you can file a complaint with HUD or your state's civil rights agency. You can also seek legal help—many disability rights organizations provide free advocacy.

You have a legal right to reasonable accommodations. A landlord cannot refuse simply because it's inconvenient or costs them money. Know your rights.

Fair Housing Act and ADA Protections

The Fair Housing Act explicitly prohibits discrimination based on disability. This means:

  • Housing programs cannot exclude you because of your disability
  • Landlords cannot charge higher rent or deposits because of disability
  • You cannot be denied housing based on stereotypes about your disability
  • Landlords must accept service animals and emotional support animals (different rules apply)
  • Housing must be accessible to people with disabilities—this includes accessible parking, elevators where needed, and accessible units

If you face discrimination, you can file a complaint with the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD). The process is free and confidential.

Section 8 Preferences for People with Disabilities

Many Public Housing Authorities give housing preferences to people with disabilities. This means you may move up the waitlist faster than other applicants. When you apply for Section 8, specifically ask if your PHA has a disability preference and if you qualify. Learn more about benefits and financial support in our Benefits & Financial Support guide.

To claim a disability preference, you'll typically need to provide documentation from a healthcare provider. But this simple step can dramatically speed up your timeline to receiving assistance.

Finding Accessible Housing

When searching for rental housing, keep these accessibility considerations in mind:

  • Building Features: Is there an accessible entrance? Are parking spaces and units accessible? Does the building have an elevator?
  • Unit Layout: Is the bathroom accessible? Can you fit mobility devices? Are doorways wide enough? Are light switches at accessible heights?
  • Appliance and Control Placement: Can you reach and operate thermostats, light switches, and appliances with your disability?
  • Yard and Entrance: Are there stairs or ramps? Is there grab support where needed?
  • Lease Terms: Will the landlord allow you to install grab bars, ramps, or other needed modifications? Will they allow a service animal?

Don't settle for housing that doesn't meet your needs. You have the right to accessible housing. If you find a place that could work with modifications, remember you can request a reasonable accommodation.

Disability Rights Organizations and Legal Help

If you face barriers to housing due to disability, you don't have to fight alone. There are organizations dedicated to helping people with disabilities navigate housing:

  • Disability Rights Organizations: Many states have federally funded protection and advocacy (P&A) programs that provide free legal help for housing discrimination
  • Legal Aid: If you have limited income, local legal aid organizations can help you file complaints and challenge unfair denials
  • Nonprofit Housing Counselors: HUD-approved housing counseling agencies can explain your rights and help with applications

How to File a Reasonable Accommodation Request

Template Letter: Here's a basic structure for your request:

"I am requesting a reasonable accommodation due to my disability [you don't need to specify the diagnosis]. This accommodation is necessary for me to have equal access to housing. The specific accommodation I'm requesting is [describe what you need]. I have attached documentation from [healthcare provider] confirming this need. Please let me know if you have questions or need additional information."

Send to: Your landlord, the PHA, or housing program in writing (email or certified mail). Keep a copy for your records.

Timeline: Landlords must respond promptly—expect a response within 5-10 business days. If denied, get it in writing and explain the reason.

If You Need Help: Contact your state's P&A program or a disability rights organization. Many provide free help with accommodation requests.

Remember Your Rights

Having a disability does not disqualify you from housing. In fact, the law requires that housing be accessible to people with disabilities. If you're facing barriers—whether it's difficulty in the application process, discrimination, or lack of accessible units—reach out to a disability rights organization or HUD. Your rights are protected.

You deserve housing that is not only available but genuinely accessible. Don't accept less, and don't hesitate to assert your rights when a landlord or program tries to deny you equal access.
