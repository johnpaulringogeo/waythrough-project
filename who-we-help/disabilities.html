Housing Resources for People with Disabilities

Disability doesn't disqualify you—it opens access to specialized housing programs and legal protections.

Section 811: Supportive Housing for Persons with Disabilities

Section 811 is a dedicated program that provides rental assistance specifically for very low-income persons with disabilities. Unlike standard Section 8, Section 811 is designed to help people with disabilities live independently in the community with supportive services tailored to their needs.

The program provides a voucher that covers most or all of your rent, paired with access to supportive services such as case management, job training, independent living skills, or other assistance based on your disability and goals. This can include mental health services, substance abuse treatment, physical therapy, or adaptive equipment.

Section 811 Eligibility

To qualify for Section 811, you must:

  • Be at least 18 years old
  • Have a disability (physical, psychiatric, developmental, or intellectual)
  • Have a very low income (generally 30-50% of area median income)
  • Meet other local program requirements

The program defines disability broadly. If you have a documented condition that substantially limits one or more major life activities, you likely qualify. This includes physical disabilities, learning disabilities, mental health conditions, cognitive disabilities, and more.

How to Apply for Section 811

Section 811 is administered through Public Housing Authorities (PHAs) in your area. Some PHAs manage it directly; others contract with nonprofits. Here's how to find and apply:

  • Contact your local PHA and ask specifically about Section 811 Supportive Housing for Persons with Disabilities
  • Ask about current applications, waitlists, and timelines
  • Be prepared to provide proof of disability (medical documentation, Social Security disability determination, etc.)
  • Complete the application and let them know about your housing and service needs
  • If accepted, you'll be assigned to a case manager who helps coordinate services and housing

Note: Like many housing programs, Section 811 sometimes has waiting lists. Apply anyway—funding does become available, and there are often special initiatives to serve particular populations.

Reasonable Accommodations: Your Right to Accessible Housing

Under the Fair Housing Act and the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), landlords, Public Housing Authorities, and housing programs must make "reasonable accommodations" to ensure people with disabilities have equal access to housing. A reasonable accommodation is a change or adjustment to a rule, policy, practice, or service that allows you to use and enjoy your home.

This is a critical protection that many people with disabilities don't know about. You don't have to accept a landlord's refusal of a service animal, an inaccessible unit, or a policy that prevents you from making necessary modifications. You have the right to request accommodations, and landlords have a legal obligation to consider them.

Types of Reasonable Accommodations

Reasonable accommodations can include:

  • Assistive Animals: Service dogs, emotional support animals, or psychiatric service animals (landlords cannot charge pet fees)
  • Accessibility Modifications: Ramps, grab bars, accessible parking spaces, widened doorways, or accessible bathrooms
  • Policy Exceptions: Allowing frequent medical appointments that might technically violate a lease clause, extra household members for caregivers, or adapted lease terms
  • Communication Accommodations: Large print leases, accessible communication formats, extra time to review documents
  • Flexibility with Standards: Modified application processes if you have difficulty filling out standard forms, flexibility on credit checks if you have medical debt

How to Request a Reasonable Accommodation

You can request a reasonable accommodation from any housing program (Section 8, public housing, private landlords, etc.) if the program or landlord is covered by the Fair Housing Act. Here's the process, and for a detailed step-by-step guide, see Request a Reasonable Accommodation:

  1. Make the Request in Writing: Send a letter or email to your landlord, PHA, or housing program. Email is fine and creates a paper trail.
  2. Be Clear About Your Disability and Limitation: Explain how your disability affects your ability to use and enjoy your home. You don't need to disclose your diagnosis, but you do need to explain the functional limitation. Example: "I have a mobility disability that makes it difficult to climb stairs. I'm requesting a ground-floor unit."
  3. Connect to Your Request: Explain the specific accommodation you need. Example: "A service animal would help me manage anxiety in public spaces, including housing."
  4. Provide Supporting Documentation: Include a letter from a healthcare provider (doctor, therapist, psychiatrist, case manager) confirming your disability and stating that the accommodation is necessary. This is important—landlords can request documentation.
  5. Follow Up: If you don't get a response in a few days, call and ask about the status. Keep records of all communications.

When a Landlord Says No

If a landlord denies your reasonable accommodation request, they must provide a legitimate reason unrelated to your disability. Common valid reasons include:

  • The accommodation would fundamentally alter the program or create an undue financial hardship
  • The accommodation would pose a direct threat to safety (narrow exception, rarely applies)

Invalid reasons include cost to the landlord, inconvenience, or general discomfort with disabilities. If you believe your request was unfairly denied, you can file a complaint with HUD or your state's civil rights agency. You can also seek legal help—many disability rights organizations provide free advocacy.

You have a legal right to reasonable accommodations. A landlord cannot refuse simply because it's inconvenient or costs them money. Know your rights.

Service Animals and Emotional Support Animals

For many people with disabilities, an animal provides essential support that makes independent living possible. Both service animals and emotional support animals (ESAs) are protected under fair housing law, but they work differently and have different protections.

Understanding Service Animals

A service animal is a dog (or in rare cases, a miniature horse) that has been individually trained to perform specific tasks related to a person's disability. These tasks might include guiding someone with blindness, alerting to a seizure, detecting low blood sugar for someone with diabetes, providing mobility assistance, or detecting a panic attack and applying pressure to ground someone with PTSD. Service animals are working animals, not pets. They're protected under both the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) and the Fair Housing Act.

Understanding Emotional Support Animals

An emotional support animal (ESA) provides comfort and emotional support through companionship. Unlike service animals, ESAs don't require specialized training—they simply need to live with you and provide calming companionship that helps manage disability-related symptoms like anxiety, depression, or post-traumatic stress. An ESA is any animal (dog, cat, bird, rabbit, etc.), but you'll need a letter from a licensed mental health provider confirming that the animal is necessary for your emotional or mental health disability.

Fair Housing Protections for Both

Both service animals and ESAs are protected under the Fair Housing Act. This means:

  • No-pet buildings must allow them. Even if a building has a strict "no pets" policy, landlords must allow service animals and ESAs in all housing.
  • No pet fees or deposits. Landlords cannot charge pet deposits, pet rent, or additional fees for service animals or ESAs. This is a legal requirement, not optional.
  • No discrimination. Landlords cannot refuse to rent to you because you have an assistance animal or charge higher rent.

What Landlords Can and Cannot Ask

Landlords CAN ask:

  • "Is this a service animal or emotional support animal required because of a disability?"
  • For service animals only: "What task or function does this animal perform?" (They're asking about the task, not the diagnosis.)
  • For ESAs: "What disability-related need does this animal address?" (Again, about the need, not medical details.)

Landlords CANNOT ask:

  • Details about your disability or medical diagnosis
  • For your medical records or mental health history
  • Proof of "certification" or "registration" (there is no official government registry for service animals or ESAs)
  • To perform specific tasks or provide a demonstration
  • For an exotic medical test or unusual certification

If a landlord asks invasive questions about your disability, that's a fair housing violation.

Documentation for ESAs

For a service animal, the animal's training and behavior usually speaks for itself. For an ESA, you'll need a letter from a licensed mental health provider—a therapist, psychologist, psychiatrist, or clinical social worker who has a professional relationship with you and understands your disability. The letter should state that you have a disability-related condition (you don't need to disclose the diagnosis) and that the animal is necessary for your emotional or mental health.

Important: Get your ESA letter from your actual treating provider—someone who has evaluated you and understands your condition. Increasingly, housing authorities and landlords are skeptical of ESA letters from online providers who have never met the patient. A letter from a telehealth provider you've worked with for a reasonable amount of time is better than a letter from a stranger, but nothing beats documentation from your own doctor or therapist.

If Your Request Is Denied

If a landlord denies your request for a service animal or ESA, you have the right to file a Fair Housing complaint. You can contact HUD at 1-800-669-9777 or file a complaint online. You can also file a complaint with your state's fair housing enforcement agency. For step-by-step guidance on filing a housing discrimination complaint, see How to File a Housing Discrimination Complaint.

Fair Housing Act and ADA Protections

The Fair Housing Act explicitly prohibits discrimination based on disability. This means:

  • Housing programs cannot exclude you because of your disability
  • Landlords cannot charge higher rent or deposits because of disability
  • You cannot be denied housing based on stereotypes about your disability
  • Landlords must accept service animals and emotional support animals (different rules apply)
  • Housing must be accessible to people with disabilities—this includes accessible parking, elevators where needed, and accessible units

If you face discrimination, you can file a complaint with the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD). The process is free and confidential.

Section 8 Preferences for People with Disabilities

Many Public Housing Authorities give housing preferences to people with disabilities. This means you may move up the waitlist faster than other applicants. When you apply for Section 8, specifically ask if your PHA has a disability preference and if you qualify. Learn more about benefits and financial support in our Benefits & Financial Support guide.

To claim a disability preference, you'll typically need to provide documentation from a healthcare provider. But this simple step can dramatically speed up your timeline to receiving assistance.

Finding Accessible Housing

When searching for rental housing, keep these accessibility considerations in mind:

  • Building Features: Is there an accessible entrance? Are parking spaces and units accessible? Does the building have an elevator?
  • Unit Layout: Is the bathroom accessible? Can you fit mobility devices? Are doorways wide enough? Are light switches at accessible heights?
  • Appliance and Control Placement: Can you reach and operate thermostats, light switches, and appliances with your disability?
  • Yard and Entrance: Are there stairs or ramps? Is there grab support where needed?
  • Lease Terms: Will the landlord allow you to install grab bars, ramps, or other needed modifications? Will they allow a service animal?

Don't settle for housing that doesn't meet your needs. You have the right to accessible housing. If you find a place that could work with modifications, remember you can request a reasonable accommodation.

Disability Rights Organizations and Legal Help

If you face barriers to housing due to disability, you don't have to fight alone. There are organizations dedicated to helping people with disabilities navigate housing:

  • Disability Rights Organizations: Many states have federally funded protection and advocacy (P&A) programs that provide free legal help for housing discrimination
  • Legal Aid: If you have limited income, local legal aid organizations can help you file complaints and challenge unfair denials
  • Nonprofit Housing Counselors: HUD-approved housing counseling agencies can explain your rights and help with applications

How to File a Reasonable Accommodation Request

Template Letter: Here's a basic structure for your request:

"I am requesting a reasonable accommodation due to my disability [you don't need to specify the diagnosis]. This accommodation is necessary for me to have equal access to housing. The specific accommodation I'm requesting is [describe what you need]. I have attached documentation from [healthcare provider] confirming this need. Please let me know if you have questions or need additional information."

Send to: Your landlord, the PHA, or housing program in writing (email or certified mail). Keep a copy for your records.

Timeline: Landlords must respond promptly—expect a response within 5-10 business days. If denied, get it in writing and explain the reason.

If You Need Help: Contact your state's P&A program or a disability rights organization. Many provide free help with accommodation requests.

Accessibility by Disability Type

Disabilities vary widely, and so do housing accessibility needs. Here's practical guidance for different disability types to help you find or request housing that works for you.

Mobility Disabilities

If you use a mobility device—a wheelchair, walker, cane, or crutches—or have difficulty with stairs and uneven surfaces, look for:

  • Ground-floor or accessible units: Avoiding stairs saves energy and reduces injury risk
  • Roll-in showers or accessible bathrooms: Grab bars, non-slip surfaces, and space to maneuver safely
  • Ramp access: At the building entrance and any step changes
  • Accessible parking: Reserved, van-accessible spaces close to the building entrance
  • Wider doorways: At least 36 inches wide to accommodate wheelchairs and scooters
  • Lever-style door handles: Easier to operate than knobs, especially if you have limited grip strength
  • Lowered countertops and light switches: Positioned at accessible heights, typically 48 inches or lower

Blind or Low-Vision Disabilities

If you navigate by touch, sound, and spatial memory, helpful features include:

  • Tactile labels on appliances: Stove, microwave, and dishwasher controls marked for touch
  • High-contrast markings: On stairs, door frames, and hazards to help with partial vision
  • Accessible thermostats: Large numbers or voice-controlled options
  • Talking smoke detectors: Alert you verbally in case of fire
  • Braille apartment numbers: On the door so you can identify your unit
  • Well-lit hallways: For people with low vision who benefit from maximum light

Deaf or Hard of Hearing

If you are deaf or hard of hearing, housing should support visual and tactile communication:

  • Visual smoke/fire alarms: Strobe lights that alert you to danger
  • Video doorbell systems: So you can see who's at the door without hearing the bell
  • Vibrating alarm clocks: Wake you up without depending on sound
  • TTY or captioned phone access: TTY phone jacks and support for video relay services
  • Visual notification systems: For building announcements and emergency alerts

Cognitive and Intellectual Disabilities

If you have a cognitive or intellectual disability, these accommodations support independence:

  • Simple lease language: Request that the landlord explain the lease in plain, straightforward language and allow extra time to review
  • Visual checklists for routines: Supported living arrangements might provide visual reminders for daily tasks like medication or cleaning
  • Supportive living arrangements: Housing with built-in support from case managers or life coaches
  • Help from case managers: Someone available to assist with rent payments, budgeting, and problem-solving
  • Proximity to treatment providers: Close to your day program, training, or job coach so transportation is manageable

Psychiatric Disabilities

If you live with a psychiatric disability like schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, or severe anxiety, consider:

  • Quiet environments: Away from noise and chaos that might trigger symptoms
  • Proximity to treatment providers: Close to your psychiatrist, therapist, or crisis services
  • Flexible lease terms: Accommodations for hospitalizations or periods when you need support
  • Reasonable accommodations for medication side effects: For example, if medication causes weight gain, request access to a gym; if it causes drowsiness, request flexible work or lease enforcement

Section 504 Accessibility Standards

Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act is a federal law that requires any program or facility that receives federal funding to provide equal access to people with disabilities. For housing, this is a powerful tool that ensures baseline accessibility standards.

What Section 504 Requires

Section 504 applies to all federally-funded housing, including:

  • Public housing (owned by PHAs)
  • Section 8 project-based assistance (where housing is reserved for the program)
  • Section 202 supportive housing for the elderly
  • LIHTC (Low-Income Housing Tax Credit) properties that receive federal funds

Under Section 504, housing providers must ensure that:

  • At least 5% of units are wheelchair-accessible with features like level entrances, accessible bathrooms, and maneuverability within the unit
  • At least 2% of units are accessible for people with hearing or vision impairments with visual alarms, accessible communication systems, and tactile or high-contrast markings
  • Common areas are accessible: Parking, entrances, hallways, laundry facilities, and community spaces must be navigable
  • Effective communication: Housing providers must communicate with you in accessible formats (large print, braille, audio, etc.)

What Happens If No Accessible Unit Is Available

Sometimes there's no accessible unit available when you need it. In that case, Section 504 says the housing provider must either:

  • Make a unit in the program accessible for you, or
  • Put you at the top of the waitlist for the next available accessible unit

This is a critical protection. You should never be told, "We'll house you in a year when an accessible unit opens." They must create or prioritize one for you.

How to File a Section 504 Complaint

If a federally-funded housing program violates Section 504, you can file a complaint with HUD's Office of Fair Housing and Equal Opportunity. You can:

  • Call HUD at 1-800-669-9777 (TTY available)
  • File a complaint online at HUD's Fair Housing website
  • Submit a written complaint to HUD's field office

Complaints are free and confidential. HUD will investigate whether the housing program is complying with Section 504 and take corrective action if needed.

When Your Accommodation Request Is Denied: The Appeals Process

If your reasonable accommodation request is denied, you have options. Here's a step-by-step process to challenge the denial and push for a fair resolution.

Step 1: Get the Denial in Writing

First, request that the housing provider give you a written explanation of why they denied your request. Ask them to state the specific reason. If they refuse to put it in writing, send an email or letter to the property manager and landlord saying, "I requested a reasonable accommodation on [date]. I did not receive a written response. Please provide a written explanation of your decision within 5 business days."

A written denial is important because it gives you documentation for any future complaint or legal action.

Common Illegitimate Denial Reasons

Watch out for these invalid reasons—they're red flags that the denial may be unlawful:

  • "It's too expensive" (cost to the landlord is not a valid reason)
  • "We don't usually do that" (inconvenience is not valid)
  • "Other tenants don't have this" (equal treatment doesn't mean identical treatment)
  • "We're worried it might cause a problem" (hypothetical concerns don't override your rights)
  • "Your disability doesn't seem that severe" (landlords cannot judge the extent of your disability)

Step 2: Write a Formal Appeal Letter

If your request is denied, send a written appeal to the property manager or housing authority. Keep it professional and factual. Here's a template:

Appeal Letter Template:

"I am writing to formally appeal the denial of my reasonable accommodation request dated [original request date]. My request was [describe the accommodation]. The stated reason for denial was [what they said].

This reason does not meet the legal threshold for denying a reasonable accommodation. My accommodation is [necessary/minimal in scope/directly related to my disability], and it would not cause [undue financial hardship/fundamental alteration/direct threat].

I am requesting that you reconsider my accommodation request within 5 business days. If you deny it again, I will file a complaint with HUD. Please provide a written response."

Send this via email or certified mail so you have proof of delivery.

Step 3: Escalate Within the Housing Provider

If the property manager denies your appeal, ask to escalate to the supervisor or the grievance process. Most housing programs (especially public housing and Section 8) have formal grievance procedures. Request information about the grievance process and follow it exactly. Document every step.

Step 4: File a Complaint with HUD

If internal appeals fail, you can file a housing discrimination complaint with HUD. You can:

  • Call: 1-800-669-9777 (TTY available for deaf/hard of hearing)
  • Online: Visit HUD's Fair Housing website
  • By mail: Contact your local HUD field office

When you file, include:

  • Your name and contact information
  • The housing provider's name and address
  • When the discrimination occurred (dates)
  • What happened and why you believe it was illegal
  • Documentation (copies of your accommodation request, denial letter, correspondence)

Important timeline: You must file a housing discrimination complaint with HUD within one year of the alleged violation. Don't wait.

Step 5: Contact Your State's Protection & Advocacy (P&A) Program

Every state has a federally-funded Protection & Advocacy (P&A) program that provides free legal help for people with disabilities facing discrimination. They can:

  • Review your case and advise you on next steps
  • Help you file an appeal or complaint
  • Represent you in negotiations with the housing provider
  • File a lawsuit if necessary

To find your state's P&A program, search "[Your State] Protection and Advocacy" or visit the National Association of Protection and Advocacy Systems (NAPSA).

Timelines and Expectations

Here's what to expect in terms of timing:

  • Housing provider response: They must respond promptly to your accommodation request. Expect 5-10 business days. If you don't hear back, that's a violation—escalate.
  • HUD investigation: Once you file a complaint, HUD must investigate within 100 days, though investigations often take longer
  • Overall resolution: Most complaints are resolved within 6-12 months, though complex cases may take longer

Documentation Is Critical

Throughout this process, keep detailed records:

  • Copies of all written requests (accommodation requests, appeals, complaints)
  • Dates and times of phone calls, and summaries of what was discussed
  • Names and titles of people you spoke with
  • Copies of all responses you receive
  • Medical documentation supporting your accommodation request
  • Any evidence of discrimination (denial letters, emails, etc.)

This documentation is your evidence. It's the difference between a vague complaint and a solid case. Keep it organized in a folder or digital file.

Remember Your Rights

Having a disability does not disqualify you from housing. In fact, the law requires that housing be accessible to people with disabilities. If you're facing barriers—whether it's difficulty in the application process, discrimination, or lack of accessible units—reach out to a disability rights organization or HUD. Your rights are protected.

You deserve housing that is not only available but genuinely accessible. Don't accept less, and don't hesitate to assert your rights when a landlord or program tries to deny you equal access.
