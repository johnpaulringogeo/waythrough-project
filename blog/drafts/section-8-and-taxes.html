Section 8 and Taxes: What You Need to Know

Matt · November 25, 2026 · 6 min read

No — your Section 8 housing assistance is not taxable income. The housing subsidy paid by the PHA to your landlord does not need to be reported on your tax return. It's treated as a government benefit excluded from gross income under IRS rules. However, there are tax-related issues that Section 8 participants should be aware of, including deductions you might qualify for and how your tax return affects your voucher.

Your Voucher Is Not Taxable Income

This is the most important thing to know: the housing assistance payment (HAP) that your PHA sends to your landlord each month is not income to you. You don't receive this money — it goes directly from the PHA to the landlord. You do not need to report it on your tax return, and it does not affect your tax bracket or your eligibility for tax credits.

The same applies to utility allowances. If your PHA gives you a utility reimbursement check (because your utility allowance exceeds your rent portion), that is also not taxable.

Tax Deductions You Might Be Missing

Even with low income, you may benefit from: the Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) — if you have earned income, this is one of the largest tax credits for low-income workers. The Child Tax Credit — up to $2,000 per qualifying child. The Saver's Credit — if you contribute to a retirement account. State-specific renter's credits — some states offer tax credits or deductions for rent paid, which may apply to your portion of rent.

These credits are refundable — meaning you can receive money even if you owe no taxes. Many Section 8 families leave money on the table by not filing a return.

How Your Tax Return Affects Your Voucher

Your PHA uses your income to calculate your rent portion. During annual recertification, they may ask for your tax return as proof of income. If your tax return shows income that you didn't report to your PHA during the year, this creates a discrepancy that could result in a retroactive rent increase or an overpayment charge.

Self-employment income is the most common area where this happens. If you do freelance work, drive for rideshare, sell online, or have any side income, report it to your PHA as it happens — don't wait for tax time.

Free Tax Preparation

You don't need to pay for tax preparation. VITA (Volunteer Income Tax Assistance) offers free tax filing for people making under $67,000/year. AARP Tax-Aide provides free tax help for anyone, with priority to people over 50. IRS Free File lets you file online for free if your income is below $84,000.

These services understand low-income tax situations and will ensure you claim every credit you're entitled to.
