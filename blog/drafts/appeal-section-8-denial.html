How to Appeal a Section 8 Denial

Matt · August 26, 2026 · 8 min read

If your Section 8 application or continued assistance is denied, you have the right to an informal hearing before an impartial hearing officer. You must request the hearing in writing within the PHA's deadline (usually 10-30 days from the denial notice). At the hearing, you can present evidence, bring witnesses, and be represented by a lawyer. Many denials are overturned at the hearing stage.

Your Right to a Hearing

Federal regulations (24 CFR § 982.554-555) guarantee you the right to an informal hearing when the PHA denies or terminates your assistance. This isn't optional — every PHA must offer this process. The hearing must be conducted by someone who wasn't involved in the original denial decision.

Common Reasons for Denial

Over income. Your household income exceeds the program limits. Appeal if you believe the income calculation was wrong or included income that should have been excluded.

Criminal history. The PHA screened your background and found disqualifying activity. Appeal if the record is inaccurate, the lookback period has passed, or you have evidence of rehabilitation.

Owing money to a PHA. A debt from previous housing assistance. Appeal if the debt amount is wrong or if you've already repaid it.

Failure to provide documents. You missed a documentation deadline. Appeal if you had good cause or if the PHA didn't properly notify you.

Program violations. From a previous tenancy. Appeal if the facts are wrong or if there were mitigating circumstances.

How to Request a Hearing

The denial letter will include instructions for requesting a hearing and the deadline. Follow them exactly. Put your request in writing — even if the PHA says you can call, always follow up in writing with a letter or email.

In your request, state that you disagree with the denial and want an informal hearing. You don't need to explain your full argument yet — save that for the hearing.

Keep a copy of your request and get proof of delivery (certified mail, email read receipt, or hand-delivery with a signed acknowledgment).

Preparing for the Hearing

Get the PHA's evidence first. You have the right to review the documents the PHA used to make the denial decision before the hearing. Request copies in writing.

Gather your evidence. Depending on the reason for denial: pay stubs and tax returns (income disputes), court records and rehabilitation certificates (criminal history), payment receipts (debt disputes), or medical documentation (reasonable accommodation requests).

Get legal representation. You have the right to bring a lawyer or advocate to the hearing. Contact your local legal aid office — many have housing law specialists who handle these cases for free.

Prepare a statement. Write out your key points so you don't forget anything under pressure. Practice explaining your situation clearly and concisely.

Bring witnesses. Employers, social workers, counselors, or others who can speak to your situation.

At the Hearing

The hearing is typically informal — not a courtroom. You'll sit at a table with the hearing officer and the PHA representative. You'll each present your side, and the hearing officer may ask questions.

Stay calm and factual. Don't get emotional or argumentative. Present your evidence clearly. Answer questions directly. If you don't know something, say so — don't guess.

After the Hearing

The hearing officer issues a written decision, usually within 1-2 weeks. If you win, the PHA must reverse the denial and proceed with your assistance. If you lose, you may have options to pursue the matter further through your state's legal system, though this requires legal counsel.

Even if you lose, the hearing creates a record. If you reapply later, having shown good faith in addressing the PHA's concerns can work in your favor.
