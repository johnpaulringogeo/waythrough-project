What to Do If Your Landlord Sells the Property While You're on Section 8

Matt · December 2, 2026 · 7 min read

If your landlord sells the property while you're on Section 8, your lease and your voucher protections don't automatically end. In most cases, the new owner must honor your existing lease for its full term. Your Section 8 Housing Assistance Payments (HAP) contract transfers to the new owner. You cannot be evicted simply because the property was sold. However, complications can arise — especially if the new owner doesn't want to participate in the Section 8 program.

Your Lease Survives the Sale

In most states, when a property is sold, the new owner steps into the shoes of the old landlord. Your lease terms remain in effect. If you have 6 months left on your lease, the new owner must honor those 6 months. They can't change your rent, change your lease terms, or evict you just because they're the new owner.

The exception: if the new owner plans to move into the unit themselves (owner-occupancy), some states allow them to terminate your lease with proper notice — usually 30-90 days. Even then, your local laws may provide additional protections.

What Happens to Your HAP Contract

The Housing Assistance Payments contract — the agreement between your PHA and your landlord — transfers to the new owner automatically. The new owner is now responsible for maintaining the unit to HQS standards and accepting the HAP payment. If they refuse to sign a new HAP contract, your PHA will work with you to find a solution, which may include helping you relocate with your voucher.

If the New Owner Doesn't Want Section 8

This is the most common problem. The new owner might want to renovate and charge higher market-rate rents. They must still honor your current lease. Once your lease expires, they can choose not to renew it and not to continue in the Section 8 program — but they must give proper notice (typically 30-60 days before lease end).

If this happens: contact your PHA immediately. Ask for a new voucher with a housing search period. Start your apartment search right away. Ask your PHA about any relocation assistance. Know that you have rights — the new owner can't force you out before your lease ends without a legal eviction proceeding and valid cause.

Protecting Yourself

If you learn your building is being sold: document the condition of your unit with photos. Make copies of your lease, HAP contract, and all correspondence with your current landlord. Contact your PHA to let them know — they should be notified of the ownership change. Don't sign any new agreements with the new owner without reading them carefully (and consulting legal aid if possible).

If the new owner tries to pressure you into leaving before your lease ends, that may be illegal harassment or constructive eviction. Contact legal aid immediately.
