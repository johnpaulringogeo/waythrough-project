Emergency Rental Assistance Programs That Still Have Funding

Matt · September 30, 2026 · 7 min read

Emergency Rental Assistance (ERA) programs were created during COVID and distributed billions in rent relief. While much of the federal funding has been spent, many state and local programs still have money — and new programs continue to launch. If you're behind on rent or facing eviction, these programs can pay months of back rent directly to your landlord. Finding them requires checking your specific city and county.

What Emergency Rental Assistance Covers

ERA programs typically cover: past-due rent (often up to 12-18 months), utility arrears (electricity, gas, water), and sometimes future rent payments for a limited period. Some programs also cover internet service and other housing-related costs. The money goes directly to your landlord or utility company — you don't receive cash.

Most programs require that you owe rent, have experienced financial hardship (job loss, reduced hours, medical expenses), and have income below a threshold — usually 80% of Area Median Income, though some programs set it at 50%.

How to Find Programs Near You

The challenge is that these programs vary wildly by location. Your city might have one that your neighboring city doesn't. Start with these sources: call 211 (the United Way helpline) and ask about rental assistance in your area. Check your city and county government websites under "housing" or "community development." Search for "[your county] emergency rental assistance" online. Contact local nonprofits like Catholic Charities, Salvation Army, and community action agencies.

Many Community Action Agencies administer ERA funds and don't advertise heavily. They're often the best-funded and least-known option.

What You'll Need to Apply

Have these documents ready before you start an application: government-issued ID, proof of income (pay stubs, tax return, benefit letters), your lease agreement, a ledger from your landlord showing what you owe, proof of the hardship that caused you to fall behind, and utility bills if you're also behind on those.

Some programs accept self-attestation for income if you can't provide documentation — especially useful if you work informally or are currently unemployed.

How Long It Takes

Processing times vary dramatically. Some programs pay within 2 weeks; others take 2-3 months. If you're facing an imminent eviction, tell the program — many have expedited tracks for urgent cases. Also let the court know you have a pending ERA application, as many courts will delay eviction proceedings to give the application time to process.

If the Federal Programs Are Spent

Even if federal ERA funding has ended in your area, alternatives exist. Many states created their own rental assistance programs with state funds. Local nonprofits run their own emergency aid funds. Some PHAs offer emergency funds to voucher holders. Faith-based organizations sometimes help with one-time rent payments. And some landlords will negotiate a payment plan directly rather than go through eviction — it's cheaper for them too.
