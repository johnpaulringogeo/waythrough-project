How Your Section 8 Rent Is Actually Calculated

Matt · December 16, 2026 · 8 min read

Your rent on Section 8 is based on 30% of your adjusted monthly income — but "adjusted" is the key word. HUD allows deductions for dependents ($480/year each), elderly/disabled households ($400/year), medical expenses (for elderly/disabled), and childcare costs. After these deductions, your Total Tenant Payment (TTP) is whichever is higher: 30% of adjusted monthly income, or 10% of gross monthly income, or the welfare rent (if applicable), or a PHA minimum rent (usually $0-$50).

Step 1: Calculate Your Annual Income

Your PHA adds up all income for every household member: wages, salaries, tips, self-employment income, Social Security benefits, SSI, SSDI, pensions, child support received, unemployment benefits, recurring financial gifts, and income from assets. Some income is excluded: earnings of children under 18, the first $480 of earnings for full-time students, certain one-time payments, and income from live-in aides.

Step 2: Calculate Adjustments

From your annual income, HUD allows these deductions: $480 for each dependent (children under 18, disabled household members, full-time students). $400 for elderly (62+) or disabled head of household or spouse. Unreimbursed medical expenses exceeding 3% of annual income (elderly/disabled households only). Reasonable childcare expenses for children under 13 that enable a household member to work, seek employment, or attend school.

These deductions reduce your adjusted income, which directly reduces your rent.

Step 3: Calculate Your Total Tenant Payment

Your TTP is the greater of: 30% of your monthly adjusted income, 10% of your monthly gross income, the welfare rent (in states that designate a housing portion of welfare), or the PHA minimum rent. Most families' TTP is calculated as 30% of adjusted monthly income.

Example: Annual gross income $24,000. Two dependents (-$960). Elderly household (-$400). Adjusted annual income: $22,640. Monthly adjusted: $1,887. TTP: $1,887 × 30% = $566/month.

Step 4: How the Voucher Covers the Rest

Your PHA's payment standard sets the maximum total rent for your voucher size in your area. If you find a unit at or below the payment standard, the PHA pays the difference between the total rent and your TTP. If the unit costs more than the payment standard, you pay the difference out of pocket — but at initial lease-up, your total housing cost can't exceed 40% of your adjusted monthly income.

Example: Payment standard is $1,500. Your TTP is $566. If you rent a unit for $1,400, the PHA pays $834 and you pay $566. If you rent a unit for $1,600, the PHA pays $934 ($1,500 - $566) and you pay $666 ($566 + $100 over payment standard).

Why Your Rent Might Be Different Than Expected

Common reasons: you forgot about a deduction you qualify for (especially medical expenses for elderly/disabled households). Your PHA made an error in the calculation (request a breakdown and check the math). Your income changed since your last recertification. The PHA's minimum rent applies. You're renting above the payment standard and paying the excess.

You have the right to request a written breakdown of your rent calculation from your PHA at any time. If you disagree, you can request an informal hearing.
