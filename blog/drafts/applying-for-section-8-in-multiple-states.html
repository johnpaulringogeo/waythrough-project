Can You Apply for Section 8 in Multiple States?

Matt · July 22, 2026 · 6 min read

You can apply to as many PHAs in as many states as you want — there's no limit and no rule against it. Each PHA maintains its own waiting list independently. Applying to multiple PHAs dramatically increases your chances of getting a voucher faster. When you reach the top of a list, you can use the voucher in any area through portability.

Why Apply to Multiple PHAs?

The average Section 8 waiting list takes 2-5 years, and some are much longer. Many lists are closed entirely. By applying everywhere you can, you multiply your chances of getting called.

And here's the key: once you receive a voucher from any PHA, you can port it to the area where you actually want to live (after the first year). So getting a voucher from a PHA in another state still helps you, even if you don't plan to live there.

How to Find Open Waiting Lists

Waiting lists open and close unpredictably. A PHA might open their list for two weeks, receive thousands of applications, then close it for years. You need to check regularly.

Affordablehousing.com and socialserve.com track open waiting lists. Your state housing finance agency website often lists current openings. HUD's Resource Locator at resources.hud.gov can help you find PHAs. Some PHAs announce openings on their social media pages.

Set up alerts and check weekly. When a list opens, apply immediately — some close within days.

The Application Process

Each PHA has its own application. Most are now online, though some still accept paper applications. You'll need basic information: household members, income, current address, and contact information.

Keep a spreadsheet tracking: which PHAs you've applied to, when you applied, your application or case number, the PHA's contact information, and when you last confirmed your place on the list.

Staying on the Lists

Most PHAs periodically purge their waiting lists. They'll send a letter (or email) asking you to confirm you're still interested. If you don't respond by the deadline, you're removed.

Keep your contact information current with every PHA. If you move or change your phone number, update every PHA you've applied to. A letter that can't reach you means you lose your spot.

What Happens When You Get Called

When you reach the top of a list, the PHA will contact you for an eligibility interview. You'll need to provide documentation: ID, Social Security cards, birth certificates, income verification, and more.

If you're called by a PHA in another state and you want to use the voucher where you currently live, tell them during the briefing. You'll go through the initial process with the issuing PHA, then port your voucher to your local PHA.

Preference Points

Many PHAs give preference to applicants who live or work in their jurisdiction. You may rank lower on an out-of-state list than local applicants. But even a lower preference position on a short list can be faster than a high-preference position on a very long one.

Common preferences: living or working in the PHA's jurisdiction, being homeless, being a veteran, being elderly or disabled, being a victim of domestic violence, and being involuntarily displaced.
