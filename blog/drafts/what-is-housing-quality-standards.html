Housing Quality Standards: The Minimum Your Apartment Must Meet

Matt · August 19, 2026 · 7 min read

Housing Quality Standards (HQS) are the minimum health and safety standards that every Section 8 unit must meet. Your PHA inspects your unit before you move in and annually afterward. If your unit fails HQS, the landlord must make repairs or risk having their Housing Assistance Payments stopped. As a tenant, HQS gives you powerful leverage to get maintenance issues addressed.

HQS Is Your Baseline Protection

HQS isn't about luxury — it's about basic safety and livability. The standard covers 13 performance areas that ensure your home has working plumbing, heating, electricity, secure doors and windows, working smoke detectors, and is free from serious hazards.

Every unit rented with a Section 8 voucher must pass an HQS inspection before the lease is approved. If it doesn't pass, the landlord must make repairs before you can move in.

Annual Inspections: Your Built-In Maintenance Tool

Your PHA inspects your unit once a year. This annual inspection is actually a powerful tool for you as a tenant. If your landlord has been neglecting maintenance, the inspector will document the violations and require repairs.

Before your annual inspection, walk through your unit and note anything that needs fixing. Make sure the inspector knows about issues like: leaking faucets, broken windows, peeling paint, non-working outlets, doors that don't lock, or pest problems.

Requesting a Special Inspection

You don't have to wait for the annual inspection. If your unit develops a serious problem, you can request a special inspection from your PHA at any time. This is especially useful when your landlord ignores repair requests.

Contact your PHA caseworker and explain the problem. They can schedule an inspection outside the regular cycle to document the condition and require the landlord to make repairs.

What Happens When a Unit Fails

If your unit fails an HQS inspection, the process goes like this:

24-hour emergencies: Life-threatening conditions (no heat in winter, gas leaks, exposed electrical wiring) must be fixed within 24 hours or the PHA can immediately abate payments.

Non-emergency repairs: The landlord typically gets 30 days to fix the issues. The PHA may grant an extension for complex repairs.

If repairs aren't made: The PHA can abate (stop) the housing assistance payments to the landlord. The landlord gets no Section 8 money until the repairs are completed and verified by re-inspection.

If abatement doesn't work: The PHA can terminate the HAP contract entirely. You keep your voucher and get a new search period to find a different unit.

Using HQS Strategically

Many tenants don't realize how much power HQS gives them. Your landlord's Section 8 income depends on passing inspections. This gives you leverage that non-subsidized tenants don't have.

If your landlord refuses to make repairs: document the problem in writing, give them a reasonable deadline, then contact your PHA for a special inspection. The threat of abated payments usually motivates action faster than anything else.

Important: your landlord cannot retaliate against you for requesting inspections or reporting HQS violations. Retaliation is a violation of federal law and your lease.
