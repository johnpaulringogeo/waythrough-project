Can You Get Section 8 With Zero Income?

Matt · August 5, 2026 · 6 min read

Having zero income does not disqualify you from Section 8. In fact, extremely low-income households are the program's priority population. With zero income, your rent portion would typically be set at a minimum rent of $0-$50 per month (set by your PHA), and your voucher would cover the rest. You'll need to document your zero-income status through a self-certification and explain how you meet basic needs.

Zero Income Is Not a Barrier

Section 8 is specifically designed for people with very low incomes. There is no minimum income requirement. If you have zero reported income, you're eligible — potentially with higher priority than applicants with some income, since the program targets those most in need.

How Rent Is Calculated With No Income

Your Total Tenant Payment is normally 30% of your adjusted monthly income. With zero income, that would be $0. However, most PHAs set a minimum rent — a floor that applies even when the formula would result in zero. The federal maximum for minimum rent is $50, and many PHAs set it lower ($25 is common).

If you can't afford even the minimum rent due to a financial hardship, you can request a hardship exemption. Qualifying hardships include: loss of benefits, waiting for a benefit application to be processed, or other circumstances beyond your control.

What You'll Need to Document

PHAs need to understand and verify your income situation. With zero income, expect to provide:

Zero income certification. A signed statement declaring you have no income from any source — wages, benefits, child support, cash contributions, or assets.

Explanation of how you're surviving. The PHA will ask how you pay for food, transportation, and other necessities. Honest answers include: staying with family, food banks, friends helping out, using savings, or receiving non-cash assistance.

Bank statements. To verify there are no deposits that would indicate unreported income.

Benefit denial letters. If you've applied for SSI, SSDI, TANF, or other benefits and been denied, bring the denial letters.

Programs to Apply for Simultaneously

If you have zero income, you likely qualify for other assistance that can help stabilize your situation while waiting for or using your voucher:

SNAP (food stamps) — available to households with little or no income. Medicaid — free health coverage for low-income individuals. TANF — temporary cash assistance, especially for families with children. SSI — if you have a disability, SSI provides monthly cash payments. General Assistance — some counties offer small monthly cash payments to very low-income adults without children. LIHEAP — help with utility bills.

These programs together can provide a foundation while you work toward stability.

Will Having No Income Raise Red Flags?

PHAs see zero-income applicants regularly. It's not unusual and it's not a red flag by itself. What can cause problems is if your reported income doesn't match what the PHA finds through verification — bank deposits, employer records in the EIV system, or state wage data.

Be completely honest about your situation. If you're doing any work — even occasional cash jobs — report it. The consequences of underreporting are much worse than reporting a small amount of income.
