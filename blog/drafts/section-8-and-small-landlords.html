A Guide for Small Landlords Considering Section 8

Matt · December 23, 2026 · 8 min read

Accepting Section 8 tenants means guaranteed partial rent from the government every month, longer-tenured tenants (Section 8 families move less often), and access to a large pool of renters. The tradeoffs: your unit must pass HQS inspection, there's paperwork to complete, and the PHA determines a "reasonable rent" that may be less than your asking price. For small landlords, the stability often outweighs the hassle — but it depends on your market.

How the Process Works for Landlords

A voucher holder finds your listing and wants to rent. They submit a Request for Tenancy Approval (RFTA) to their PHA with your unit's details and your asking rent. The PHA performs a rent reasonableness determination — comparing your rent to similar unassisted units in the area. The PHA schedules an HQS inspection of your unit. If the unit passes inspection and the rent is approved, you sign a lease with the tenant and a HAP contract with the PHA. The PHA pays their portion directly to you every month, usually by direct deposit.

The Inspection

HQS inspections check for basic health and safety: working plumbing, heating, electricity, smoke detectors, secure doors and windows, no lead paint hazards (pre-1978 buildings), structurally sound condition, and adequate living space. These aren't luxury standards — they're minimum habitability requirements that any decent rental should meet.

If your unit fails, you'll be given a list of items to fix and a deadline (usually 30 days for non-emergency items). Once you've made repairs, the PHA re-inspects. Annual inspections continue as long as the tenancy lasts.

What You Get Paid

The PHA determines a "payment standard" for each bedroom size in their jurisdiction. If your rent is at or below the payment standard, the PHA pays the difference between the tenant's portion (their TTP) and the total rent. You receive one payment from the tenant and one from the PHA each month.

The PHA also conducts a rent reasonableness test to ensure your rent isn't higher than comparable unassisted units. If your asking rent is too high, the PHA will tell you what they'll approve, and you can negotiate or decline.

Real Advantages for Small Landlords

Guaranteed income: the PHA's portion arrives every month regardless of the tenant's personal situation. Lower turnover: Section 8 tenants tend to stay longer because finding another voucher-friendly landlord takes effort. Larger applicant pool: there are far more voucher holders looking for housing than there are landlords accepting vouchers. Accountability: if there's a problem with the tenant, the PHA can mediate — and in serious cases, the tenant risks losing their voucher.

Common Concerns (and Reality)

The inspection seems invasive — it's really just checking basic habitability. Most well-maintained units pass on the first try. Paperwork is annoying — there is paperwork, but it's frontloaded. After the initial lease-up, the process is mostly automated. Section 8 tenants damage property — studies show no difference in property damage rates between voucher and non-voucher tenants. The rent is too low — in some high-demand markets, yes, the payment standard may be below market rate. In moderate markets, it's often competitive.
