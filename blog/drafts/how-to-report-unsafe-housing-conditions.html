How to Report Unsafe Housing Conditions

Matt · November 18, 2026 · 7 min read

Every tenant — whether on Section 8 or not — has the right to housing that meets basic health and safety standards. If your landlord won't fix dangerous conditions like mold, pests, lack of heat, broken plumbing, or structural hazards, you can report them to your local code enforcement office, your PHA (if you're on Section 8), and your local health department. Retaliation for reporting is illegal in every state.

What Counts as Unsafe Conditions

Federal Housing Quality Standards (HQS) and most local codes require: working plumbing with hot and cold water, functioning heating system, working electricity with no exposed wiring, structurally sound walls, floors, and ceilings, working smoke detectors, secure windows and doors with functioning locks, no lead paint hazards (especially in homes built before 1978), no severe pest infestations, no mold or persistent moisture problems, and adequate ventilation.

If any of these are missing or broken and your landlord refuses to fix them, that's reportable.

Step 1: Document Everything

Before reporting, create a paper trail. Take dated photos and videos of the problem. Write down when the problem started and when you first noticed it. Save copies of any written requests you've made to your landlord. Keep a log of phone calls (date, time, who you spoke to, what was said). If the condition is making you or your family sick, get medical documentation.

This documentation protects you if the landlord retaliates and strengthens any complaint you file.

Step 2: Notify Your Landlord in Writing

Send a written request for repairs — even if you've asked verbally multiple times. Use email, text, or a letter. Be specific: describe the problem, state that it affects your health/safety, and request repair by a specific date (usually 14-30 days, depending on your state). Keep a copy. This written notice is often legally required before you can take further action.

Step 3: Report to the Right Agency

For Section 8 tenants: call your PHA and request a complaint inspection. Your unit must meet HQS at all times, not just during annual inspections. If the PHA finds violations, they give the landlord a deadline to fix them — and can abate (stop) the landlord's housing assistance payment until repairs are made.

For all tenants: contact your local code enforcement or building inspection department. They'll inspect and can issue violations that legally compel the landlord to make repairs. Contact your local health department for mold, lead paint, or pest issues specifically.

Protection Against Retaliation

Every state prohibits landlord retaliation for reporting housing code violations. Retaliation includes: eviction or threats of eviction, rent increases, reducing services, harassment, or refusing to renew your lease. If you experience retaliation after reporting, document it and contact a tenant rights attorney or legal aid organization.

On Section 8, your PHA can also intervene if your landlord retaliates — they have a vested interest in the unit being maintained properly.
