You Owe a Housing Authority Money — Now What?

Matt · July 29, 2026 · 7 min read

If you owe money to a housing authority — usually from an overpayment, unreported income, or damages — it can prevent you from receiving housing assistance until the debt is resolved. Most PHAs will set up a repayment agreement allowing you to pay the balance over time. Once you've paid in full or have an active repayment agreement in good standing, you can typically reapply for assistance.

How PHA Debts Happen

The most common reasons people owe a PHA money:

Overpayment of housing assistance. If your income was higher than what you reported, the PHA paid more than it should have. The difference is your debt.

Unreported income. If you earned income you didn't report during recertification, the PHA will recalculate what your rent should have been and bill you the difference.

Damages beyond wear and tear. If you left a PHA-owned unit (public housing) with damages, you may owe repair costs.

Unpaid rent. Back rent owed from a previous tenancy in public housing or other PHA-managed property.

How It Affects Your Housing

An outstanding PHA debt typically means you'll be denied if you apply for housing assistance — whether at the same PHA or a different one. PHAs check the Enterprise Income Verification (EIV) system, which tracks debts owed to any PHA nationwide.

This means you can't just apply to a different housing authority to avoid the debt. It follows you.

Setting Up a Repayment Agreement

Contact the PHA you owe and ask about a repayment agreement. Most PHAs will work with you on a reasonable payment plan based on your income. Monthly payments of $25-$100 are common for people with limited income.

Get the agreement in writing. Make every payment on time. Keep receipts and records of every payment. Once you've completed the repayment or are in good standing, get written confirmation that your debt is resolved.

Disputing the Debt

If you believe the debt is incorrect — wrong amount, wasn't your fault, or based on inaccurate income calculations — you have the right to dispute it.

Request a detailed accounting. Ask the PHA to show exactly how the debt was calculated. They must be able to document it.

Request a hearing. You have the right to an informal hearing to dispute the debt. Present any evidence that the amount is wrong or that the situation was beyond your control.

Get legal help. Legal aid can review the PHA's calculations and represent you at a hearing. Errors in overpayment calculations are surprisingly common.

After the Debt Is Resolved

Once you've paid off the debt or completed your repayment agreement, get written documentation from the PHA confirming the balance is zero. Keep this letter permanently — you may need it when applying for housing assistance in the future.

Some PHAs may still deny you for a period after the debt is resolved, but many will consider a completed repayment as evidence of responsibility. Having the documentation ready strengthens any future application.
